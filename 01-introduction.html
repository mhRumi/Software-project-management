01 · Why does this matter?

Software projects fail — constantly10 min

These aren't made-up numbers. They come from real government audits and independent surveys of real software projects. Start here — because the question isn't whether this happens. It's why it keeps happening, even when the teams are smart and the budgets are big.

£2.3bn
UK government ICT spend in 2002–03 — more than the roads budget that year
82%
of 13,322 IT projects surveyed were late (Standish Group, 2003)
43%
of those same projects came in over budget

The National Audit Office investigated the failures and found one root cause: "lack of skills and a proven approach to project management and risk management." Not bad technology. Not small budgets. The absence of structure around how the work was managed.

02 · What is a project?

Not everything is a project15 min

Paying staff every week is a routine task — same steps, same people, same outcome, repeated indefinitely. Moving from a manual process to a new payroll system? That's a project. The word sounds simple, but the distinction matters a lot for how you manage the work.

Think of it as a spectrum. At one end, pure routine. At the other end, pure exploration. Projects live in the middle — not so familiar that you can run on autopilot, not so unknown that you can't even set a goal.

Routine jobs ← left end of the spectrum
You do these the same way every time. Same inputs, same process, predictable outputs. A payroll run. A weekly report. The goal of management here is efficiency — do the known thing faster and with fewer errors.
Projects ← the middle
Non-routine work with a clear objective, a fixed time limit, and constrained resources. You need to plan because you haven't done exactly this before. The goal of management here is delivery — reach the objective without running out of time, money, or people.
Exploration ← right end of the spectrum
You're not sure what you're looking for yet. Research, R&D, breakthrough innovation — the outcome is genuinely uncertain, and you might discover the goal itself changes as you work. Managing exploration looks very different from managing a project.
Eight things that make something a "project"

Not all of these have to apply at full intensity, but you'll find most of them in any real project:

Non-routine
This exact combination of goal, context, and constraints hasn't been tackled before. You can't just copy last time's playbook.
Requires planning
Because it's non-routine, you have to think ahead — you can't just rely on habit or standard procedure.
Specific objectives
There's an agreed end state that defines "done." Without it, the project never ends — it just evolves forever.
Fixed time span
Projects have a start and a finish. They're not ongoing operations.
Work for someone else
There's a client or stakeholder who commissioned it and will judge whether it succeeded.
Multiple specialisms needed
No single person has all the skills required. You need a team.
Temporary team
People are brought together for the project's duration, then dispersed. Managing temporary teams is very different from managing a permanent department.
Constrained resources
Budget, time, and people are all limited. Trade-offs are unavoidable — and the PM's job is to make them explicitly, not accidentally.

03 · What makes software different?

Four things that make software uniquely hard to manage15 min

Fred Brooks managed IBM's OS/360 in the 1960s — one of the most ambitious (and troubled) software projects in history. From that experience, he identified four properties that make software projects harder to manage than almost any other kind of engineering work. Once you see them, you'll spot them everywhere.

INV
Invisibility
Walk past a building site and you can immediately tell if it's 20% done or 80% done — the structure is right there. Walk past a software team and you see the same screens whether they've built almost nothing or almost everything. Software progress is invisible. This makes it very easy to be confidently wrong about how much has actually been done.
CPX
Complexity
A Boeing 747 has around 6 million physical parts. A modern operating system can have billions of possible states it can be in — and even a simple app, once you account for all combinations of inputs and user actions, has more complexity than most physical products. Unlike a car, you can't look at the parts and visually check whether they fit together correctly.
CON
Conformity
A civil engineer can say "physics doesn't allow that" and end the conversation. A software developer can't. Requirements come from people — and people are inconsistent, contradict themselves, and change their minds. The software has to conform to whatever humans need, even when those needs conflict with each other.
FLX
Flexibility
"Can we just add one more feature?" With a bridge, that question sounds absurd. With software, it sounds reasonable — it's just code, right? This expectation of easy change is what quietly doubles the scope of a project while the deadline stays the same. Software can be changed, so people expect it to absorb every new idea that comes up.
One more distinction worth making
Client-side PM
Manages the budget, contract, and relationship with the supplier. Asks: "Are we getting what we paid for?" This person works for the organization that commissioned the software.
Supplier-side PM
Manages the people doing the actual build — the team, the schedule, the quality. This is the focus of this course. You'll be looking at software management from the inside.
04 · The software project lifecycle

Every project goes through the same journey15 min

The details vary enormously between projects, but the high-level shape is always the same. The book shows it at two levels of detail:

Figure 1.2 — the high-level cycle
Three questions in sequence: Is it worth doing? (feasibility study) → How do we do it? (planning) → Do it (execution). Teams that skip the first two questions almost always regret it — they end up building the wrong thing, or building it without a plan.
Figure 1.3 — ISO 12207 (the detailed version)
Requirements analysis → Architecture design → Detailed design → Code & test → Integration → Qualification testing → Installation → Acceptance support. This is a reference standard, not a rigid recipe. Chapter 4 will look at when to follow it closely and when to use something more flexible.

05 · Plans, methods, methodologies

Three terms that get mixed up constantly10 min

In industry these words get used interchangeably. They shouldn't be — they mean different things, and confusing them leads to planning mistakes.

Method
A technique for doing a type of task
Like a recipe — it's general and reusable. "Code review" is a method. "Function-point analysis" is a method. A method tells you how to approach a category of work, but not the specifics for this particular job.
Plan
A method applied to a specific situation
A plan is concrete — real names, real dates, real resources. It's never generic. "We will do code review, led by Fatima, every Friday at 10am, starting March 5th" is a plan. The same project, with different people and a different deadline, would have a completely different plan.
Methodology
A collection of related methods used together
Agile is a methodology — it groups sprints, standups, retrospectives, and user stories into one coherent approach. Scrum is a methodology. PRINCE2 is a methodology. Picking a methodology means picking a whole family of methods that were designed to work together.
How do you choose the right approach?

Projects can be characterized along a few key dimensions — and the right methodology depends heavily on where your project sits:

Compulsory vs. voluntary users
Employees using an internal system have no choice — they must use whatever you build. A consumer app has to compete for adoption. This changes the quality bar and the importance of usability completely.
Information system vs. embedded/real-time
An information system manages data and business processes. An embedded system controls physical hardware in real time — a pacemaker, a car's braking system. The testing and reliability requirements are in a completely different league.
Objective-driven vs. product-driven
Objective-driven: the goal is clear ("reduce payroll processing time by 50%") but the exact solution isn't defined yet. Product-driven: the deliverable is specified in detail before work begins. Most projects start objective-driven and drift toward product-driven as design decisions are made.
Quick categorization — Brightmouth College (Exercises 1.4–1.5)

Apply these three dimensions to Brightmouth College's payroll project. Is it objective-driven or product-driven? Information system or embedded? Compulsory or voluntary users? Be ready to justify each answer — there's more room for debate than it first appears.

06 · Stakeholders and objectives

More people have a stake than you think15 min

Most people assume a project has one client. In reality, there are almost always multiple groups with different — and sometimes contradictory — interests. Every one of those groups has the power to make your project fail, not by attacking it, but simply by withdrawing their support.

Internal to the project team
The developers, analysts, testers — the people doing the work. They want interesting problems, reasonable workloads, and to learn something. Ignore them and the best ones leave.
External to the team but inside the organization
Finance, HR, other departments, senior management — anyone in the same organization who'll be affected by what you build. They want it delivered on time, on budget, and actually useful for their work.
External to the organization
Customers, suppliers, regulators, the public. Their requirements may be legally non-negotiable. Surprising a regulator late in a project is very expensive.

Theory W — Boehm & Ross: Your job as a project manager is to make every stakeholder a winner. Not a compromise where everyone loses a little bit equally — genuinely win. Because anyone who loses will eventually find a way to make the project lose too: by changing requirements, withdrawing approvals, or simply not championing the work when it needs support.

SMART objectives — turn wishes into commitments

"Improve the system" is not an objective — it's a wish. A real objective is testable. The SMART framework is how you know whether you've actually written one:

S
Specific
Name the exact outcome. Not "improve performance" — "reduce payroll processing time from 4 hours to 2 hours."
M
Measurable
If you can't measure it, you can't know when you've achieved it — and you can't defend your answer if someone disagrees.
A
Achievable
Ambitious is fine. Impossible destroys morale. The target has to be reachable with the available people, budget, and time.
R
Relevant
Does this objective connect to something the organization actually needs? A technically perfect feature that nobody uses isn't relevant.
T
Time-constrained
Without a deadline, "eventually" becomes the default answer to "when will this be done?" A deadline forces prioritization.

07 · Project success vs. business success

You can win the project and lose the war10 min

Here's one of the most important ideas in the whole course: a project can be delivered on time, on budget, and exactly to spec — and still be considered a failure.

Imagine you've been asked to build an app that employees use to book meeting rooms. You ship it on time, within budget, zero bugs, full feature list. Three months later, nobody's using it — everyone still just sends a quick message to the receptionist. You hit every project target. The business objective — getting people to actually change how they book rooms — was never achieved.

Project objectives
Functionality delivered. On time. On budget. To the agreed quality. These are internal success criteria — within the PM's direct control. Hitting all four doesn't guarantee business success.
Business objectives
Does the delivered system create real value? Do people use it? Does it change behaviour? These are external criteria — often outside the PM's direct control, but they're the reason the project existed in the first place.
Discussion prompt

"Can you think of a product that was delivered on time, on budget, to spec — but failed in the market?" Take 2–3 examples from the class. This is a question you'll have much richer answers to by the end of this course than you do right now.

08 · What does a PM actually do?

Eight activities and one feedback loop10 min

People often describe project management as vague — "keeping things on track" or "communicating." Here's a more precise answer: a PM performs eight distinct activities, continuously, throughout the project.

Planning
Decide what needs to happen, when, and by whom. Not once at the start — continuously. Plans get revised as reality diverges from what was expected.
Organizing
Break the work into pieces. Assign the right pieces to the right people. Make sure everyone understands how their piece fits into the whole picture.
Staffing
Get the right people. Keep them. Help them develop. The team is the project — get this wrong and everything else is harder.
Directing
Make decisions when the team is stuck. Resolve conflicts before they slow things down. Keep work moving forward when the path is unclear.
Monitoring
Collect real data on what's actually happening — not what you hoped would happen, and not what people tell you when they're trying to sound positive. No monitoring = no control.
Controlling
Act on what monitoring tells you. Adjust the plan. Reallocate resources. Monitoring without control is just watching things go wrong.
Innovating
Find better ways to work. Don't just repeat what the last project did. Adapt methods to what this particular project actually needs.
Representing
Be the interface between your team and the outside world. Manage stakeholder expectations on both sides — protect the team from unnecessary noise, but make sure the organization stays informed.
The project control cycle — Figure 1.4

Control isn't a one-time action — it's a loop that runs continuously: collect data from the real world → compare to the plan → decide what to do → implement the decision → observe the world again. Projects fall out of control when teams stop cycling through this loop — usually because monitoring feels uncomfortable when things aren't going well.

Exercise 1.9 — Paul Duggan's day

The book gives a short story about a section manager's typical Monday: a staffing meeting, a team member's accident, finding a replacement contractor, a difficult call with a client, reviewing a progress report.

Map each of Paul's actions to one of the 8 management activities. Then compare with a partner. You'll find the same action often maps to more than one activity — that's the point. Management activities don't happen in isolation.

09 · Wrap-up

Five things to take away from today5 min

1. Software projects are uncertain by nature
Not because teams are careless — because invisibility, complexity, conformity, and flexibility are built into the medium. Design your management approach around that uncertainty, don't pretend it away.
2. Clear objectives are non-negotiable
Without them, every person on the team has a slightly different definition of "done" — and nobody realizes until near the deadline.
3. Objectives must be measurable
"Better performance" is a wish, not an objective. If you can't measure it, you can't manage it — and you can't prove you achieved it.
4. Project success ≠ business success
On time + on budget + to spec is not the full definition of success. The system also has to actually solve the problem it was built for.
5. Communication needs to be set up deliberately
Problems don't surface unless there are channels for them to surface through. Silence from a team is never a good sign — it usually means people don't feel safe raising issues, not that everything is fine.
