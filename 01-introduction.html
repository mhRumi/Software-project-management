01 · Why does this matter?

Software projects fail — constantly10 min

Open with the book's own numbers — no need to import outside statistics here. These figures come from the chapter itself (§1.2) and the impact hits immediately.

£2.3bn
UK government ICT spend in 2002–03 — more than it spent on roads that year
82%
of 13,322 projects surveyed by the Standish Group (2003) were late
43%
of the same projects came in over budget
Root cause (National Audit Office)

Failures were traced to "lack of skills and proven approach to project management and risk management." Not technology. Not budget. The absence of structured management.

Discussion prompt — open the session

"Why do IT projects fail so often, even with smart people and big budgets?" Give students 2 minutes to write down one reason, then take 3–4 responses before moving on.

02 · What is a project?

All projects are about meeting objectives15 min

§1.1: the simplest possible definition — a project is a planned activity. Software project management = ensuring stakeholder objectives are met within the context of that planned activity.

Figure 1.1 — the project spectrum

Draw this on the board (or show it from the text). Projects sit between two poles:

Routine jobs ← left end
Repetitive, low uncertainty. Same inputs, same outputs, every time. Example: processing weekly payroll with an existing system.
Projects ← middle
Non-routine, require planning, have specific objectives, a fixed time span, and constrained resources. This is where the course lives.
Exploration ← right end
High uncertainty — outcomes can't be defined in advance. Example: a research programme or a Mars rover mission where the "product" isn't fully known at the start.
Eight characteristics that mark something as a "project"

From §1.3 — run through these quickly; the point is to distinguish projects from both pure routine work and open-ended exploration:

1. Non-routine
Something that hasn't been done in exactly this form before.
2. Requires planning
The non-routine nature means you must think ahead — you can't rely on habit.
3. Specific objectives
There is an agreed-upon end state that defines completion.
4. Fixed time span
Projects have a start and an end — they are not ongoing operations.
5. Work for someone else
There is a client or stakeholder who commissioned the work and will judge its success.
6. Multiple specialisms
Different types of skill are required — no single person has all of them.
7. Temporary team
People are brought together for the duration of the project, then dispersed.
8. Constrained resources
Budget, time, and people are limited — trade-offs are unavoidable.
Exercise 1.1 — in-class activity (5 min)

Rank these examples along the routine → project → exploration spectrum and justify your ordering:

  • Producing a daily newspaper
  • A Mars rover mission
  • Getting married
  • A second-year programming assignment
  • Installing a word processor update

Good opener — gets discussion going, and students often disagree on the edge cases (the programming assignment is particularly interesting).

03 · What makes software projects different?

Brooks' four characteristics15 min

From §1.4. Fred Brooks identified four properties of software that make its project management uniquely difficult compared to building a bridge or manufacturing a product. These recur throughout the entire course.

INV
Invisibility
Physical progress on a bridge is visible — you can see it grow. Software progress isn't. A team might be 80% done or 20% done and it looks the same from the outside. This makes monitoring and control uniquely hard.
CPX
Complexity
Software has more complexity per pound/dollar than virtually any other engineered product. A large system can have billions of possible states — no other engineering discipline deals with this scale of combinatorial complexity.
CON
Conformity
Software must conform to human and organizational requirements, which are inconsistent, contradictory, and constantly changing. Unlike physics, there's no underlying natural law the software can appeal to — it just has to do what people want.
FLX
Flexibility
Because software can be changed easily (compared to concrete), it is expected to change — constantly. The perceived malleability of software becomes a source of uncontrolled scope growth.
§1.5 — Contract vs. technical project management

This book focuses on supplier-side PM (managing the people who build the software). Briefly distinguish:

Client-side PM
Manages the budget, contract, and relationship with the supplier. Concerned with "are we getting what we paid for?"
Supplier-side PM
Manages the technical delivery — the team, the schedule, the quality. This is the primary focus of this course.
04 · The software project lifecycle

From feasibility to acceptance15 min

§1.6. Two figures to work through — draw both on the board or refer to the book:

Figure 1.2 — the high-level cycle
Feasibility study ("is it worth doing?") → Plan ("how do we do it?") → Project execution ("do it!"). A project can loop back — feasibility might be revisited mid-execution as scope changes.
Figure 1.3 — ISO 12207 lifecycle
Requirements analysis → Architecture design → Detailed design → Code & test → Integration → Qualification testing → Installation → Acceptance support. Note: this is a reference standard, not a prescription — Chapter 4 will discuss when to follow it strictly and when to adapt it.
Running case study — introduce here (Exercise 1.2)

Brightmouth College currently has its payroll run by a local authority computer centre. The college wants to replace this with an off-the-shelf package run in-house.

Ask students: What are the main stages of this project? How would it differ if the software were written from scratch instead of purchased?

This case study recurs throughout §1.8–1.16 and will be used as the running example for the entire semester. It reappears in Chapters 2–13. Introduce it now and make it familiar.

05 · Plans, methods, methodologies

Three terms students confuse10 min

§1.7–1.8. Clarify these three terms precisely — they are used loosely in industry but the book distinguishes them carefully:

Method
How to do a type of task
A general technique applicable to a category of task. Example: "structured walkthrough" is a method for reviewing code.
Plan
A method applied to a specific real situation
A plan takes a method and gives it concrete activities, dates, owners, and resource allocations. It's always particular — not generic.
Methodology
A grouped set of methods
A collection of related methods used together. Example: object-oriented design is a methodology (it groups together multiple related methods for analysis, design, and implementation).
Categorization dimensions (§1.8)

Projects can also be characterized along these axes — useful for choosing the right methodology (Chapter 4 goes deeper):

Compulsory vs. voluntary users
Payroll users have no choice but to use the system. A consumer app must compete for user adoption. This affects quality and usability requirements dramatically.
Information systems vs. embedded/real-time
An IS project manages data and business processes. An embedded/real-time system must respond to the physical world within tight time constraints — testing and reliability requirements are very different.
Objective-driven vs. product-driven
Objective-driven: the goal is clear but the solution isn't yet defined (e.g. "reduce payroll processing time by 50%"). Product-driven: the deliverable is specified in detail upfront (e.g. a specific piece of software with defined features).
Exercises 1.4–1.5 — quick categorization

Apply these categories to Brightmouth College's payroll project: Is it objective-driven or product-driven? Is it an information system or an embedded system? Are the users compulsory or voluntary?

Students should be able to justify each answer — the point is to practice applying the framework, not to get a single "right" answer.

06 · Stakeholders and objectives

Who has a stake, and what do they want?15 min

§1.9–1.10. This is one of the most practically important sections. Students often assume a project has one client — in reality there are multiple parties with conflicting interests, and the PM's job is to manage all of them.

Three categories of stakeholder
Internal to the project team
Developers, analysts, testers — people doing the work. They want reasonable workloads, interesting problems, career development.
External to the team but inside the organization
Finance dept, HR, management, other departments who will use or be affected by the system. They want it delivered on time, on budget, and actually useful.
External to the organization
Customers, suppliers, regulatory bodies, the public. Their requirements may be non-negotiable (e.g. legal compliance).
Theory W — Boehm & Ross

Win-win: The project manager's job is to make every stakeholder a winner. If any party loses, they will eventually find a way to make the project lose too — by withdrawing support, changing requirements, or undermining decisions. Identify what "winning" means for each stakeholder group early.

SMART objectives

Project objectives must be testable. "Improve performance" is not an objective — it's a wish. The SMART framework turns wishes into measurable commitments:

S
Specific
Targets a particular outcome, not a vague direction. "Reduce payroll processing time" → "Reduce payroll processing time by 50%."
M
Measurable
Can be objectively verified. If you can't measure it, you can't know whether you've achieved it.
A
Achievable
Realistic given the constraints. Setting an impossible target destroys team morale and project credibility.
R
Relevant
Connects to an actual business need. Objectives that don't link to real organizational goals will eventually lose stakeholder support.
T
Time-constrained
Has a deadline. Without one, "eventually" becomes the default answer to "when will this be done?"
Exercises 1.6–1.8 — group activity (8 min)

In groups of 3–4, using Brightmouth College's payroll project:

  • Exercise 1.6: List all the stakeholders you can identify — external, internal, and within the project team
  • Exercise 1.7: Identify a project authority — who should have the final say on scope and acceptance?
  • Exercise 1.8: Write two or three SMART objectives for the project. Challenge: can you write one for Brigette (the project leader) and one for the Finance department?
07 · Business case and project success

What does it mean to succeed?10 min

§1.11–1.12. This distinction is one of the most important in the entire course and the book places it here in Chapter 1 deliberately.

Project objectives
Functional requirements delivered. On time. On budget. To the specified quality. These are the internal success criteria — within the PM's direct control.
Business objectives
Does the delivered product actually create value for the organization? Does it get used? Does it change behaviour? These are external success criteria — often not within the PM's control but absolutely matter.

A project can hit all four project-objective targets and still be a business failure. Shipped on time, on budget, to spec — and nobody uses it. This is the business case failure: the project solved the wrong problem, or the problem changed, or the benefits were never realized.

Discussion prompt

"Can you think of a product that was delivered on time, on budget, to spec — but still failed in the market?" Take 2–3 examples from the class. This is where the lecture naturally opens up to student experience.

08 · What is management?

Eight activities and the control cycle10 min

§1.13–1.14. "Management" is often treated as a vague concept. This section gives it concrete structure — 8 specific activities that a PM performs, and a feedback model for how control actually works.

The 8 management activities
Planning decide what to do and how
Deciding what will be done, when, by whom, and with what resources. This is not a one-off event — plans are revised continuously.
Organizing structure the team and work
Dividing work into tasks, assigning responsibilities, establishing reporting lines.
Staffing get the right people
Recruiting, training, and retaining the people needed for the project.
Directing guide day-to-day work
Giving instructions, making decisions, resolving conflicts as work proceeds.
Monitoring watch what is happening
Collecting data on progress, quality, and cost — without monitoring you have no basis for control.
Controlling act on what you see
Taking corrective action when monitoring reveals a deviation from plan. Control without monitoring is guesswork; monitoring without control is useless.
Innovating find better ways
Adapting methods and processes as you learn more about the project and its constraints.
Representing interface with the outside
Communicating with stakeholders, sponsors, clients, and other teams — managing the project's external relationships.
Figure 1.4 — the project control cycle

Draw this loop on the board:

Real world → data collection → data processing (compare to plan, model outcomes) → decisions (revise plan, reallocate resources) → implementation → back to the real world. Projects are controlled by continuously cycling through this loop — not by writing a plan once at the start and executing it blindly.

Exercise 1.9 — Paul Duggan case (5 min)

The book gives a short narrative of a section manager's typical day: a staffing meeting, a team member's accident, arranging a replacement, dealing with a client call, reviewing a report.

Students map each of Paul's actions to one of the 8 management activities. Works well as a quick individual exercise before comparing answers as a class — the same action often maps to multiple activities, which is the interesting part.

09 · Wrap-up

Five conclusions and a template5 min

§1.15–1.16. The book states 5 conclusions for the chapter — worth stating them explicitly:

1. Projects are inherently uncertain
Especially software projects — the invisibility and flexibility characteristics mean change is constant.
2. Software has particular difficulties
Invisibility, complexity, conformity, and flexibility make software PM harder than most other engineering disciplines.
3. Clear objectives are central to success
Without them, the team has no shared target and stakeholders have no basis for evaluating progress.
4. Objectives need measurable tests
SMART criteria — if you can't measure it, you can't manage it.
5. Communication channels must be established early
With all stakeholders — not just the development team. Surprises late in a project are almost always caused by communication failures early on.
Annex 1 — the project plan template
Capstone tie-in

The book's Annex 1 gives the standard sections of a project plan: Introduction, Background/business case, Objectives, Constraints, Methods, Products, Activities, Resources, Risks, Management.

This is the template for the semester capstone project plan deliverable. Point it out now — students will be filling this in incrementally as each chapter is covered. Every chapter in this course contributes something to one or more of these sections.
