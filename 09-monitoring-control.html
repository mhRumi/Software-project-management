Monitoring & Control

01 · Getting real data

The hardest part is getting honest information15 min

The project control cycle from Week 1 (observe → process → decide → act) only works if the "observe" step produces accurate data. But people under pressure systematically report optimistic progress — especially when they think bad news will be unwelcome. If your team knows that "I'm behind" leads to micromanagement, they'll say "nearly there" until they can't anymore. Then the problem surfaces at the worst possible moment.

The PM's job is to create conditions where honest reporting is safe and expected — and to triangulate multiple data sources so self-reporting isn't the only input.

Progress reports
Regular written updates from each team member or work package owner: what was completed, what's in progress, what's blocked, what's ahead. The format should be standardized and lightweight — if it takes more than 15 minutes to write, it won't get done honestly.
Product inspection
Review the actual deliverables, not just the status reports. Code exists or it doesn't. Specifications are approved or they're not. Don't accept "90% done" as a status — what specifically is the remaining 10%?
Meetings
Checkpoint meetings, standups, reviews. The value isn't the meeting itself — it's the conversations that happen around it. Team members who won't write down a problem often talk about it in a 1-on-1. Regular face-to-face check-ins surface problems the formal reporting misses.
Exception reports
When something significant deviates from plan — budget, schedule, scope, quality — a formal exception report goes to senior management with: what happened, the impact, options, and a recommendation. Exceptions should be rare but communicated immediately, not collected for the monthly report.

The 90% problem: "I'm 90% done" is the most dangerous phrase in software project management. The last 10% almost always takes longer than the first 90%. When you hear "nearly done," ask: what specific tasks remain? What's the estimated effort for each? Have you hit any problems in that remaining work? Replace percentage claims with concrete deliverable status.

02 · Earned Value Management

Three numbers that tell you exactly where you stand30 min

Traditional progress tracking has a flaw: it measures cost and schedule separately. A project can be within budget and behind schedule — because it's done less work than planned. Earned Value Management (EVM) integrates cost and schedule into a single coherent framework.

EVM is based on three values for each activity and for the whole project:

PV
Planned Value (Budgeted Cost of Work Scheduled)
How much work should have been done by now, expressed in budget terms. This is read from your baseline plan — at week 8, the plan says we should have completed work worth £24,000 of budget. PV = the plan.
EV
Earned Value (Budgeted Cost of Work Performed)
How much work has actually been done, in the same budget terms. If a £10,000 activity is 60% complete, it has earned £6,000. EV = what you've actually accomplished, measured in plan-dollars.
AC
Actual Cost (Actual Cost of Work Performed)
What you've actually spent to achieve the EV. If you spent £8,000 to earn £6,000 of value, you're over cost. AC = what you've actually spent.
Derived metrics
CV
Cost Variance = EV − AC
Positive = under budget. Negative = over budget. CV = EV − AC.
SV
Schedule Variance = EV − PV
Positive = ahead of schedule. Negative = behind schedule. SV = EV − PV.
CPI
Cost Performance Index = EV ÷ AC
CPI > 1.0: getting more than £1 of work per £1 spent. CPI < 1.0: over budget. A CPI of 0.85 means you're getting 85p of work for every £1 spent.
SPI
Schedule Performance Index = EV ÷ PV
SPI > 1.0: ahead of schedule. SPI < 1.0: behind. SPI of 0.80 means you've done 80% of the work that should be done by now.

EVM's most powerful feature: forecasting. Given the current CPI, what will the project cost at completion? EAC = BAC ÷ CPI. If the total budget (BAC) was £80,000 and CPI is 0.71, the forecast final cost is £80,000 ÷ 0.71 = £112,676. That's an early warning — when the project is only 25% done — that it will overrun by £32k if nothing changes.

03 · Milestone slip charts

Visualizing schedule drift over time10 min

A milestone slip chart tracks, over time, how the forecast completion date for each milestone changes. Each row is a milestone. Each column is a reporting date. The cells show the forecasted completion date for that milestone at that point in time. If the forecast slides right over successive reports, the schedule is slipping.

The chart makes a pattern obvious that gets hidden in narrative reports: a milestone that's "still on track" for 5 reports in a row and then suddenly slips 4 weeks is actually a failure of early warning — the slip was happening all along, but nobody surfaced it until it was unavoidable.
