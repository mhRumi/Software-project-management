Software Quality

Your app passes every test. It deploys without errors. The PM says it's done. Then users find it impossible to learn, it crashes under load, and the team can't update it without breaking other things. Quality is much bigger than "no bugs." This week: what quality actually means, how to plan for it, and how organizations improve their capability to deliver it.

01 · What quality actually means

Quality is conformance to requirements — and fitness for purpose10 min

In 2003, a NASA Mars probe called the Mars Climate Orbiter was lost because one engineering team used metric units and another used imperial. The software was technically correct for its own components. But the system as a whole was wrong. Every component passed its tests. The mission failed.

Quality in software isn't just about the absence of bugs in individual components. It's about whether the entire system does what stakeholders actually need — reliably, efficiently, and in a way they can use.

Quality assurance (QA) → process-focused
Ensuring that the processes used to build software are appropriate and are being followed correctly. QA looks at how the work is being done — audits, reviews, process checklists. Prevention-focused: catch problems before they create defects.
Quality control (QC) → product-focused
Testing and inspecting the software itself to find defects. QC looks at what has been produced. Detection-focused: find defects that have already been created and fix them before release.

The most important quality principle: the cost of finding and fixing a defect rises dramatically the later it is caught. A requirement error found in review costs pennies to fix. The same error found in user acceptance testing costs 10–100× more. Found in production: potentially orders of magnitude more. Quality is not just a moral good — it's economically rational.

02 · ISO 9126 quality model

Six ways software can be high or low quality20 min

ISO 9126 (now succeeded by ISO 25010) defines six top-level quality characteristics. Each breaks down into measurable sub-characteristics. This gives teams a concrete vocabulary for defining quality requirements — not just "the software should be good" but "the software should have a mean time between failures of at least 500 hours under peak load."

03 · The quality plan

Quality doesn't happen by accident — it needs a plan15 min

A software quality plan (SQP) is a project document that defines: what quality means for this project, how it will be measured, and what processes will be used to achieve it. It's written at the start of the project, not retrofitted at the end.

QP1
Quality targets
Specific, measurable quality goals for this project. Not "the software should be reliable" but "the system shall have 99.5% uptime during business hours and recover from failure within 30 seconds." Each ISO 9126 characteristic relevant to this project gets a target.
QP2
Product and process standards
Which coding standards, design conventions, review procedures, and testing standards apply. This is where team-specific rules are documented — naming conventions, mandatory peer review, static analysis thresholds.
QP3
Reviews and audits
When and how work products will be reviewed. Who reviews what, at which milestone, against what criteria. Formal technical reviews (FTR) are planned events, not ad-hoc inspections. Walkthroughs (informal, author leads) vs inspections (formal, checklists, independent moderator).
QP4
Testing strategy
Unit testing, integration testing, system testing, acceptance testing — which types, who does them, what the exit criteria are. Not just "we will test the software" but a structured plan that can be evaluated.
QP5
Change control
How defects and change requests are tracked, prioritized, and resolved. Without a process here, fixes break other things and the quality baseline erodes silently.
04 · Verification and validation

"Are we building it right?" vs. "Are we building the right thing?"10 min

These two questions look similar but address completely different risks. Barry Boehm's classic formulation is the clearest way to remember the distinction.

Verification → "are we building it right?"
Checking that the software conforms to its specification. Internal consistency check. Does the code do what the design says? Does the design do what the requirements say? Techniques: code review, static analysis, inspections, unit tests checked against specs.
Validation → "are we building the right thing?"
Checking that the software meets the actual needs of users and stakeholders — not just the specification. Reality check. The specification might be wrong. The requirements might be incomplete. Techniques: user acceptance testing, prototype feedback, usability testing, demos to real users.

The V-Model maps verification to each development stage and its corresponding testing phase: requirements → acceptance test, architectural design → integration test, detailed design → unit test. Each left-side activity produces a testable artifact that the corresponding right-side activity verifies. This is why requirements must be testable — if you can't write a test for a requirement, it's not a requirement, it's a wish.

05 · CMM and process improvement

Organizations, like software, can be measured and improved15 min

The Capability Maturity Model (CMM), developed at the Software Engineering Institute, describes five levels of software process maturity. Most organizations sit at level 1 or 2. Reaching level 3 is considered the threshold of genuine process discipline. Levels 4 and 5 are rare and require sustained organizational investment.

CMM is applied to the organization, not individual projects. It describes how consistently and repeatably the organization can deliver software, regardless of which team or which project.

1
Initial — heroic effort
Processes are ad hoc and unpredictable. Success depends on individual heroics. The same kind of project may succeed spectacularly one time and fail badly the next, depending on who's doing it. No consistent process, no lessons learned between projects.
2
Repeatable — project management in place
Basic project management processes are established. Requirements management, project planning, configuration management. Previously successful practices can be repeated on similar projects — but only similar ones. Novel projects still at high risk.
3
Defined — standard processes across the organization
A defined, documented set of software engineering and management processes is used organization-wide. Projects tailor the standard processes for their context rather than inventing from scratch. A baseline everyone works from. Most quality frameworks aim for level 3 as a minimum.
4
Managed — quantitative measurement
Both the software process and products are measured and controlled quantitatively. Defect rates, productivity, quality targets — all tracked with data. Management decisions based on data, not intuition. Process performance is predictable within statistical bounds.
5
Optimizing — continuous improvement
Quantitative feedback is used to continuously improve processes. The organization systematically identifies weaknesses, experiments with improvements, and adopts what works. Defect prevention is built into the process. Rare — requires sustained leadership commitment and years of investment.

CMMI (Capability Maturity Model Integration) is the successor to CMM — it integrates process improvement for software, systems engineering, and services into one framework. The levels are the same; the scope is broader. Many government contracts now require suppliers to be CMMI Level 3 certified as a minimum.

Group Discussion — 10 min

Consider a small software agency with 20 people. They deliver roughly 8 projects per year. Each project is estimated and planned differently. Some have code reviews, most don't. They've never done a retrospective that was actually followed up on.

1. What CMM level are they probably at? What's your evidence?

2. What is the single highest-value change they could make to improve their maturity level?

3. Why might moving from Level 1 to Level 2 be harder than it sounds?

06 · Testing approaches

Different kinds of tests catch different kinds of problems10 min

Unit testing
Tests individual functions or modules in isolation. Fast, cheap, can be fully automated. Catches logic errors in components but says nothing about how components interact.
Integration testing
Tests that components work together correctly. Uncovers interface mismatches, timing issues, data format mismatches — the exact class of problems that unit tests can't see. The Mars Climate Orbiter was an integration failure.
System testing
Tests the complete, integrated system against the full requirements spec. Includes performance testing (does it meet load targets?), security testing, reliability testing — quality characteristics beyond pure function.
Acceptance testing
Performed by or with the customer/user. Validates — not just verifies — that the system meets actual needs. The final gate before release. In agile: sprint reviews are lightweight acceptance tests every iteration.
Regression testing
Re-running tests after changes to ensure that fixes haven't broken something that was previously working. The reason automated test suites are so valuable — running 3,000 regression tests manually before each release is impractical.
