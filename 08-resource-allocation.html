Resource Allocation

A schedule that ignores people is a fantasy. This week we learn how to take a network diagram and a Gantt chart — and actually staff them with real people who have limited hours and other commitments.

01 · What resources are

People are the obvious resource — but not the only one10 min

When people say "we don't have enough resources," they almost always mean people. But a complete resource plan must account for everything the project consumes:

Labour → the dominant resource in software
People, measured in time. Not interchangeable — a senior developer and a junior developer are not the same resource type. Skill-matching to activities is essential. The wrong person on a critical activity is often worse than no person at all.
Equipment & facilities
Development machines, test environments, server capacity, licenses, physical space. In cloud-based projects, test environment cost can be significant. Book shared resources early — the test environment is often a bottleneck shared across multiple projects.
Materials
Less relevant for pure software, but important for embedded systems (hardware prototypes), training materials, or data migration tools that must be procured.
Time itself
Calendar time is a resource. The project plan must account not just for effort-hours but for elapsed time — public holidays, leave periods, the fact that a 5-day task assigned to a part-time team member takes 10 calendar days.
02 · Resource histograms

Making over-allocation visible20 min

A resource histogram shows how many units of a resource are required in each time period. The x-axis is time (weeks or months). The y-axis is the resource demand (number of people, or hours). The limit line shows what's actually available. When bars exceed the limit, you have over-allocation — something that has to be resolved before the plan is viable.

Max: 3 devs
Over-allocated Within capacity
What you do when bars exceed the limit
Resource smoothing → keep the deadline, adjust resource usage
Use float on non-critical activities to move demand from peaks to valleys. Delay a non-critical task (within its float) from a busy week to a quieter one. The project end date doesn't move — you're just redistributing work within the available slack. This is always preferable to leveling when there's enough float.
Resource leveling → keep the resource limit, allow the deadline to slip
When there isn't enough float to smooth the peaks, you delay activities even beyond their float — which pushes back the project end date. Leveling gives you a realistic schedule but at the cost of delivery date. This is the honest trade-off: if the team can only have 3 developers, and the plan demands 5, the end date must move.

Cost-schedule trade-off: adding resources (more people, overtime, contractors) to avoid leveling costs money. The PM must weigh: is the cost of keeping the original deadline less than the business cost of missing it? Sometimes the answer is obviously "pay the overtime." Sometimes the business case doesn't support it — and the honest answer is to replan.

03 · Brooks' Law

"Adding manpower to a late software project makes it later"15 min

Frederick Brooks documented this in The Mythical Man-Month (1975), and it remains one of the most important — and most frequently violated — laws in software project management.

When a project is late and management's first instinct is "add more people," here's what actually happens:

Training overhead
New team members must learn the codebase, the domain, the team's conventions, and who to ask for what. During this ramp-up period — which can take weeks — they consume existing team members' time rather than adding productive capacity.
Communication complexity grows non-linearly
A team of 4 has 6 communication channels. A team of 8 has 28. A team of 12 has 66. Each new person added requires n−1 new communication paths. The coordination overhead starts to dominate the productive work.
Work packages must be re-divided
Tasks that one person was handling must now be split between two — which requires interfaces between the pieces, coordination time, and integration work that didn't exist before. You create new work by adding people.

When does adding people help? It helps when: the project is genuinely parallelizable (adding people to independently-scoped workstreams), the new people are already familiar with the codebase, and there's enough time left for the ramp-up cost to be recovered. It almost never helps in the last 20% of a project — which is exactly when management is most tempted to try it.

04 · Costing the schedule

Turning a schedule into a budget15 min

Once resources are allocated to activities, you have the information to build a cost schedule — week by week, who is doing what, at what rate. This becomes the basis for financial reporting and for earned value analysis (covered in Week 9).

Direct vs. indirect costs
Direct: labour hours × rate, purchased equipment, external contractors. Directly attributable to specific activities. Indirect: overhead — rent, management time, support functions. Allocated across the project as a percentage of direct cost. Together they form the total project cost.
The S-curve
When cumulative cost is plotted against time, most projects follow an S-shape: slow spend in early phases (planning, requirements), accelerating spend during build, slowing again as testing and closeout consume fewer resources. Deviations from the expected S-curve in monitoring are an early warning sign — covered in Week 9.
Contingency reserve
Budget set aside for identified risks that materialize. Typically 10–25% of direct cost, based on the risk assessment from Week 7. This is different from management reserve (for unknown unknowns) — contingency is planned and justified by the risk register.
