Managing Contracts

Most real software projects involve contracts — with clients, with suppliers, with both. A contract is an alignment tool: it defines what each party expects and what happens when expectations aren't met. Get this wrong and technical success won't save you.

01 · Contract types

Which party takes the risk?20 min

A contract type is fundamentally a decision about who carries the financial risk of uncertainty. If requirements are clear and scope is fixed, the supplier can take the risk. If requirements are fuzzy and scope will change, the client must accept more financial exposure. There's no universally "better" type — it depends entirely on how much is known at signing time.

Contract typeWho takes the riskBest forMain danger
Fixed price Supplier — they absorb cost overruns Well-defined requirements, stable scope, experienced supplier Supplier over-estimates to cover risk, or cuts corners to protect margin if scope was underestimated
Time & Materials (T&M) Client — they pay for all actual hours Agile projects, exploratory work, when scope will evolve No natural cost ceiling — the client is exposed to unlimited cost growth
Cost plus fixed fee Client pays actual cost + agreed fee R&D, novel work where cost genuinely can't be predicted Supplier has little incentive to control costs — they earn the same fixed fee regardless
Cost plus incentive fee Shared — supplier earns more for coming in under budget/time Long-term relationships, large complex projects Complex to negotiate and administer — defining "incentive" requires good metrics
Fixed price per unit Shared — price is set per deliverable Maintenance, support services where volume is variable Disputes over what counts as a "unit" of work; quality can drop as supplier optimizes for volume

The connection to project approach: Fixed-price contracts assume scope is defined — which maps to waterfall. T&M contracts accommodate changing scope — which enables agile. If a client insists on fixed-price for an agile project, something has to give: either the agile approach or the fixed price. This mismatch is the source of enormous contract disputes in industry.

02 · Selecting a supplier

The cheapest bid is rarely the best deal20 min

When choosing between suppliers, the temptation is to anchor on price. The better approach: define your requirements first, weight each requirement by importance, score each supplier against them, and see who comes out ahead on the weighted total. Price is one criterion among many — not the only one.

Invitation to Tender (ITT)
The document you send to potential suppliers describing what you need. Should include: scope, technical requirements, quality standards, timelines, reporting expectations, and evaluation criteria. The ITT sets the terms of the comparison — if you don't specify it, you can't fairly compare responses to it.
Evaluation criteria
Weighted against: technical capability, relevant experience, proposed approach, team quality, price, references, financial stability. Evaluating bids without pre-defined criteria leads to post-hoc rationalization — you know which supplier you liked and you find reasons to pick them.
Pilot / proof of concept
For high-value or high-risk contracts, ask shortlisted suppliers to build a small proof of concept. The cost of the pilot is trivial compared to the cost of choosing the wrong supplier for a year-long engagement. Real capability always beats claimed capability.
03 · What a contract contains

Handshakes don't resolve disputes15 min

A good contract isn't adversarial — it's a shared memory of what was agreed, so that when disputes arise (and they will), there's a reference point rather than two competing recollections. Every software contract should address:

Deliverables and acceptance criteria
What will be delivered? What tests or criteria determine if it's acceptable? Without acceptance criteria, "done" is subjective — and the supplier will use their definition while the client uses theirs.
Payment terms
When are payments due? Milestone-based payments (tied to accepted deliverables) are usually better than time-based payments for the client. For the supplier, milestone payments create risk — they may do work before being paid. Both sides need this negotiated explicitly.
Intellectual property
Who owns the code, the data, the design? Does the client have source code escrow rights? Can the supplier reuse components in future projects? These questions are easy to ignore and expensive to resolve in court.
Liability and warranties
What happens if the software causes a loss? Who is liable, up to what limit? What warranty period applies after delivery? Without these, the supplier's implied liability may be unlimited — which they will price into their bid, or refuse the contract entirely.
Variation procedure
How are changes to scope handled? Who can authorize a change? How is additional cost agreed? Without a variation procedure, every change request becomes a dispute. This is especially critical under fixed-price contracts.
Termination conditions
Under what conditions can either party exit? What notice is required? What payments are due on termination? Having this written down makes it much less likely to be needed.
04 · Managing the relationship

The contract is the floor, not the ceiling10 min

Good contract management isn't about enforcing the contract — it's about making the contract unnecessary by maintaining a healthy working relationship. The best supplier-client partnerships treat the contract as a last resort, not a daily reference document.

Regular communication
Scheduled progress meetings, shared dashboards, joint working sessions. Don't let the client's only view of the project be the monthly report — by then, surprises are unavoidable. The PM on the supplier side has dual accountability: to their own organization, and to the client relationship.
Change control
Every request for change should go through a formal process: describe the change, estimate the impact, agree on cost and schedule adjustment, get written approval before work begins. The most common source of project failure isn't technical — it's scope creep that was never formally acknowledged or paid for.
Managing the end
Project closeout requires: formal acceptance sign-off, handover of documentation, knowledge transfer, ensuring warranties are understood, and a final review of lessons learned. Projects that fade out without formal closure often resurface as disputes when the client discovers something didn't work as expected six months later.
