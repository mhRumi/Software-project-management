Effort Estimation — Algorithmic Models

Can a formula tell you how long a software project will take? Not perfectly — but the right formula, applied with judgment and calibrated to your organization, gets you a lot closer than gut feeling alone.

01 · Algorithmic models

A formula built from thousands of past projects10 min

An algorithmic model is built by collecting data from a large set of completed projects — size, effort, duration, team size, complexity — and fitting a mathematical formula to the patterns. You then feed your current project's characteristics into the formula and get an estimate back.

The best-known family of models is COCOMO (Constructive Cost Model), developed by Barry Boehm in 1981 and updated as COCOMO II in the 1990s. It's based on data from 63 projects at TRW Aerospace. The basic insight: effort grows non-linearly with size — doubling the size of a project more than doubles the effort, because coordination and communication costs increase faster than the code does.

The crucial limitation up front: all algorithmic models are calibrated on historical data from other organizations. If your team, technology, and domain are similar to the calibration set, the model may be accurate. If not, it can be wildly off. The model gives you a starting point and a structure for thinking — not a certified answer. Always triangulate with other methods.

02 · COCOMO I Basic

Three project types, two equations20 min

COCOMO I Basic classifies every project into one of three categories based on complexity, and applies a different formula to each. "Size" is measured in Thousands of Delivered Source Instructions (KDSI) — not ideal, but this was 1981. (COCOMO II updates this to Function Points and Object Points.)

Organic → small teams, well-understood domain
Examples: batch data processing, simple business applications, familiar technology. The team has done similar work before. Most organizational software projects fall here at the smaller end.
Semi-detached → mixed experience, medium complexity
Mix of experienced and inexperienced staff. Some novel requirements. Typical business systems, utility software, transaction processing systems.
Embedded → tight constraints, novel/complex
Strong performance, reliability, or safety requirements. Operating systems, compilers, real-time control systems. Teams facing significant technical unknowns.
The COCOMO I Basic formulas
Organic: Effort = 2.4 × KDSI^1.05 person-months
Semi-det: Effort = 3.0 × KDSI^1.12 person-months
Embedded: Effort = 3.6 × KDSI^1.20 person-months
Duration: Time = 2.5 × Effort^0.38 months (similar exponents vary by type)

The exponent > 1 is the key insight. For Organic projects, the exponent is 1.05 — effort grows slightly faster than size. For Embedded projects, it's 1.20 — effort grows much faster than size. This captures the "diseconomy of scale" in software: large projects don't just take proportionally more time, they take disproportionately more time because of coordination overhead, integration complexity, and communication costs.

03 · COCOMO II

The 1990s update — built for modern software15 min

COCOMO I was built for the 1970s–80s world: mainframes, batch processing, measured in source lines of code. By the 1990s, object-oriented programming, reusable components, rapid application development, and modern tooling had changed what "building software" meant. COCOMO II was calibrated on 161 projects and designed to handle these realities.

Three sub-models
Application Composition model: for prototyping and applications built with high-level tools (4GLs, GUI builders). Uses Object Points as the size measure. Early Design model: for use before architecture is set. Uses Function Points. Post-Architecture model: the main detailed model, used once the system architecture is established. Most accurate, requires the most information.
Scale factors (replacing the 3-type classification)
COCOMO II replaced COCOMO I's three project types with five scale factors, each rated 1–6: Precedentedness (how similar to prior work?), Development flexibility, Architecture/risk resolution, Team cohesion, Process maturity. These determine the exponent applied to size — which determines how much worse large size makes things.
Effort multipliers (cost drivers)
17 factors (down from 15 in COCOMO I, reorganized) that multiply or reduce the base estimate: product reliability requirements, database size, personnel capability, tool maturity, schedule pressure. Each rated Very Low / Low / Nominal / High / Very High / Extra High, with calibrated multiplier values.
The COCOMO II Post-Architecture formula
Effort = A × Size^E × Π(EM)
where A = 2.94 (calibration constant)
E = 0.91 + 0.01 × ΣSF (exponent from scale factors)
EM = product of all effort multipliers

04 · Putting it all together

Use multiple methods and triangulate10 min

No single estimation method is reliable on its own. The professional approach is to use at least two or three methods and compare the results:

Bottom-up (from WBS) + COCOMO + Function Points
If all three methods give results in the same ballpark, confidence is higher. If they diverge significantly, find out why — usually one method is using incorrect inputs or the project has unusual characteristics that distort one of the models.
Range estimates, not point estimates
Rather than "this project will take 12 months," say "our estimates range from 9 to 16 months across three methods, with a central estimate of 12 months, contingent on [stated assumptions]." Commit to the range, not the point.
Calibrate to your organization
COCOMO and function point benchmarks are based on industry averages. Your team's productivity, your tools, and your domain will differ. Track actuals on every project and adjust your multipliers over time. After 5–10 projects, your organization-specific calibration will be far more accurate than any industry model.
