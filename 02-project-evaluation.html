01 · The business case

Before a team is assembled10 min

§2.1–2.2. Recap from last week: every project needs a justification before resources are committed. The business case is the formal document that provides this justification.

The 10-part business case structure (§2.2)
1. Introduction / Background
Why is this proposal being made? What context prompted it?
2. Proposed project
What will be built or changed? High-level scope.
3. Market
Who are the customers/users? What is the demand?
4. Organizational / operational infrastructure
What organizational changes are needed alongside the technical ones?
5. Benefits
What value will be created — and for whom? (Covered in depth in §2.4–2.5.)
6. Outline implementation plan
High-level schedule — when will this be done?
7. Costs
Development, setup, and ongoing operational costs over the system lifetime.
8. Financial case
The cost-benefit analysis — is this financially justified?
9. Risks
What could go wrong with the project or the expected benefits?
10. Management plan
Who owns this initiative and how will it be governed?
Capstone tie-in

This 10-part structure is what capstone teams will produce as their first deliverable — pairs naturally with the Annex 1 project plan template from Week 1. Announce this now so students can start thinking about it.

02 · Project portfolio management

Choosing which projects to run10 min

§2.3. An organization doesn't run just one project — it manages a portfolio of projects simultaneously. Portfolio management is the process of deciding which projects to invest in, and in what priority.

Three aspects of portfolio management
Define
Portfolio definition
What projects are currently in flight? What is proposed? An accurate inventory is the starting point — many organizations are surprised by how many projects they're actually running.
Manage
Portfolio management
Prioritizing, resourcing, and coordinating the active set of projects. Trade-offs when resources are constrained.
Optimize
Portfolio optimization
Balancing the mix — new product development (NPD) vs. renewal projects (improving how the organization operates internally).

The "below the line" problem: informal, ad-hoc work that doesn't appear in the official portfolio but still consumes real staff time and budget. Every organization has it; most underestimate it. Getting this work into the portfolio is part of what portfolio management achieves.

03 · Evaluating individual projects

Technical and cost-benefit assessment10 min

§2.4. Before committing to a project, two questions must be answered:

Technical assessment
Can the required functionality be achieved with current, affordable technology? If not, either the approach or the requirements must change. This is often the first filter applied.
Cost-benefit analysis
Identify all costs (development, setup, operational over lifetime) and all benefits — then express both in monetary terms. Figure 2.1 shows the typical cash flow shape: negative during development, positive once the system is operating, sometimes negative again at decommissioning.
Exercise 2.1 — Brightmouth College (in-class)

For the Brightmouth College payroll project, students identify and categorize all costs and benefits:

  • Quantified and valued — can be expressed as a specific monetary figure
  • Quantified but not valued — measurable but not easily converted to money (e.g. "reduces errors by 30%")
  • Identified but not easily valued — real benefits that resist quantification (e.g. "improved staff morale")

Good discussion: which benefits are easiest to quantify? Which are most likely to be underestimated?

04 · Cost-benefit evaluation techniques

Four ways to measure financial value20 min

§2.5. Work through Table 2.1 (four IOE project cash flows) live — ideally on a spreadsheet on a projector so students can follow the arithmetic. Each technique reveals something different about the same set of cash flows.

Technique What it measures Formula / approach Key limitation
Net Profit Total income minus total costs over project life Σ income − Σ costs Ignores when cash flows occur — £50k in year 1 and £50k in year 5 look the same
Payback Period How long until cumulative income = total cost (break-even) Read from the cumulative cash flow table Ignores everything that happens after the break-even point
ROI / ARR Average annual profit as a percentage of total investment (avg annual profit ÷ total investment) × 100 Still ignores timing; can't compare with bank interest rates directly
NPV Present value of all future cash flows, discounted by interest rate PV = value ÷ (1 + r)ᵗ — then sum all years Requires a chosen discount rate; sensitive to that choice
IRR The discount rate that makes NPV = 0; a comparable "% return" Solve iteratively (or use a spreadsheet's IRR function) Doesn't show absolute size of return; can have multiple solutions for complex cash flows
NPV worked example — Table 2.3, Project 1

Walk through this at 10% discount rate. The formula for each year:

PV of year t cash flow = net cash flow ÷ (1 + 0.10)ᵗ
NPV = Σ (all years' PV) = £618 (positive → worth doing at 10%)

Key takeaway to state explicitly: no single technique is sufficient on its own. Real evaluation uses several together — net profit checks viability, payback period checks how long you're exposed to risk, ROI allows board-level comparison, NPV accounts for the time value of money and re-ranks projects that look equal on simpler measures.

Exercises 2.2–2.6 — follow along with Table 2.1

Have students calculate payback period (Ex 2.3), ROI (Ex 2.4), and NPV (Ex 2.5–2.6) for the IOE projects using their own calculators or a shared spreadsheet. The key insight emerges from Ex 2.5–2.6: NPV re-ranks Projects 1 and 2 even though they have identical net profit — because NPV is sensitive to when the money comes in.

05 · Risk evaluation

Probability, expected value, and decision trees10 min

§2.6. Distinguish two types of risk that are easy to conflate:

Project risk
Threatens the successful execution of the project — delays, cost overruns, technical failures. This is managed by the PM during the project. (Covered in depth in Week 7.)
Business risk
Threatens whether the delivered product will be profitable. Even a perfectly executed project can fail if the market changes, competition undercuts prices, or the expected benefits don't materialize. This is the focus here.
Risk matrix — Table 2.5

Rate each business risk on two dimensions: importance (H/M/L — how bad if it happens?) × likelihood (H/M/L — how probable?). Risks in the HH cell need immediate mitigation strategies.

Handling risk: discount rate adjustment

For risky projects, add a risk premium (typically 2–5%) to the discount rate used in the NPV calculation. This automatically reduces the NPV of uncertain future benefits — a higher discount rate means future cash flows are worth less in today's money.

Exercise 2.7 — BuyRight expected income

Three possible income scenarios with assigned probabilities. Students calculate expected income = Σ(income × probability) using Table 2.6. Then ask: should BuyRight proceed, given development costs of £750,000 and ongoing operational costs of £200,000/year?

Figure 2.2 — Decision tree (discuss)

A sales order system: extend vs. replace, under uncertainty about market expansion. Walk through calculating the expected value of each branch: Extend = £40,000, Replace = £10,000 → choose Extend. Decision trees make implicit trade-offs explicit and auditable.

06 · What is a programme?

When projects need each other10 min

§2.7. Sometimes a strategic objective can't be achieved by a single project. A programme is a coordinated group of related projects managed together to achieve benefits that couldn't be realized if each project were managed independently.

Ferns' definition

"A group of projects that are managed in a coordinated way to gain benefits that would not be possible were the projects to be managed independently."

Five types of programme
Business cycle programmes
Tied to an annual planning cycle — the portfolio of projects funded in a given fiscal year.
Strategic programmes
Implement a specific strategic goal: e.g. all projects contributing to a digital transformation initiative.
Infrastructure programmes
Renew shared platforms — network upgrades, data centre migrations, ERP implementations — that underpin multiple other projects.
R&D programmes
Open-ended investigation — objectives may evolve as learning accumulates.
Innovative partnerships
Cross-organizational — two or more organizations co-invest in a shared goal.
07 · Programme manager vs. project manager

Different jobs, different mindsets10 min

§2.8–2.9. Table 2.7 from the book. This is a comparison worth dwelling on — students often assume "programme manager" is just a senior project manager. The role is genuinely different:

Programme Manager Project Manager
Manages many simultaneous projectsManages one project at a time
Personal relationship with skilled individual resourcesImpersonal relationship with a resource type (e.g. "a Java developer")
Goal: maximize resource utilization across the portfolioGoal: minimize resource demand for their project
Projects tend to be similar (domain expertise matters)Projects tend to be dissimilar across a PM's career
Focuses on benefit realization over yearsFocuses on delivery within a fixed time box

08 · Creating a programme

From mandate to delivery plan10 min

§2.10. Walk through the pipeline using the book's "merging two organizations" example:

01
Programme mandate
Senior management decides a strategic change is needed. This triggers the programme. A Programme Director is appointed.
02
Programme brief
High-level definition: vision statement, expected benefits, known risks, rough costs and timescales.
03
Blueprint
The "to be" state in detail: business models, organizational structure, information systems required, costs of the new state.
04
Benefit profiles
For each benefit: what it is, how it will be measured, when it will be realized, and who owns it.
05
Programme portfolio
The set of projects needed to move from current state to the blueprint. Then: preliminary plan + financial plan.
09 · Aids to programme management

Dependency diagrams and tranches10 min

§2.11. Two tools for visualizing how projects within a programme relate to each other:

Dependency diagram — Figure 2.3
Shows which projects must complete before others can begin. The book's example has 7 projects (A through G) where both C and D depend on A completing first — A is a "systems study" that both subsequent workstreams need before they can start. Visualizes the critical sequencing constraints.
Delivery planning / tranches — Figure 2.4
Groups the portfolio into tranches — sets of projects that deliver a coherent package of benefits together. Each tranche ends with a decision gate: continue to the next tranche, or stop? This makes the programme reviewable and stoppable at logical points.
10 · Reservations and benefits management

Did the benefits actually arrive?5 min

§2.12–2.14. The hardest part of programme management is ensuring that the benefits described in the business case are actually realized after the projects complete.

Types of benefit (Table 2.8)
Mandatory compliance
Legal or regulatory requirement — the benefit is avoiding a penalty or maintaining the right to operate.
Quality of service
Faster, more reliable, more accurate service delivery.
Productivity
Doing more with the same (or fewer) resources.
Risk reduction
Reducing the probability or impact of adverse events.
Economy
Reducing costs.
Revenue enhancement / acceleration
Increasing revenue, or getting to revenue faster.
Strategic fit
Positioning the organization better for future opportunities.
Disbenefits

Not all consequences of a change are positive. Disbenefits are negative outcomes that should be identified and managed — e.g. more sales driven by a new system → more overtime cost for the fulfilment team.

Business change managers own benefit realization after the project closes. This is the crucial insight: benefits can't be tracked within a project's own lifetime — they emerge during operations, months or years after delivery. Someone outside the project team must be accountable for ensuring benefits are realized.

Reservations (§2.12)

A programme isn't a "super-project." Over-structuring risks creating bureaucracy that prevents the individual projects from responding to change. Programmes must evolve as the business environment changes — hold the structure loosely.

11 · Wrap-up

Four conclusions (§2.14)

1. Projects must be evaluated on multiple grounds
Strategic fit, technical feasibility, and economic return — all three, not just the financial case.
2. Many projects are only justifiable as part of a programme
A project whose benefits can't be realized independently needs a programme to combine it with enabling projects.
3. Not all benefits can be precisely quantified
Some genuine value — improved morale, strategic positioning, reduced risk — resists financial quantification. A good business case acknowledges this rather than ignoring non-quantifiable benefits.
4. Assess costs and income over the full system lifetime
Not just development costs. Operational costs over 5–10 years often dwarf the initial build cost — NPV forces this discipline by requiring all future cash flows to be identified.
