Project Evaluation & Programme Management

How do you know whether a project is worth doing? And once you've said yes, how do you manage twenty projects that all need each other? This week, we answer both questions.

01 · The business case

The question your boss will always ask10 min

Imagine walking into your manager's office and saying: "I need eight people, a full year, and £500,000." The first thing they'll say — before anything else — is "Why?"

The business case is your answer to that question. It's the document that justifies a project before a single resource is committed. Every project needs one. Skipping it is how organizations end up building things nobody wanted.

The 10-part business case structure (§2.2)

This is also your capstone Deliverable 1 template — start thinking about which sections you can fill in for your chosen scenario right now.

1. Introduction / Background
What situation prompted this proposal? Why now?
2. Proposed project
What will actually be built or changed? Keep it high-level — the details come later.
3. Market
Who are the users or customers? What demand exists? This section matters more for commercial products than internal systems.
4. Organizational / operational infrastructure
What needs to change in the organization — not just the technology — for this to work? New roles? Changed processes? Retraining?
5. Benefits
What value will this create, and for whom? Covered in detail in §2.4–2.5 — this is where the financial case lives.
6. Outline implementation plan
A high-level schedule. Not yet a real project plan — just enough to show the proposal is achievable in a plausible timeframe.
7. Costs
Development, setup, and ongoing operational costs — across the full lifetime of the system, not just what it costs to build.
8. Financial case
The cost-benefit analysis. Is this worth the investment? (Exactly what we'll practice in §2.5.)
9. Risks
What could go wrong — during the project, or with the expected benefits afterwards?
10. Management plan
Who owns this? How will it be governed? Who makes the big decisions?
Capstone tie-in

This 10-part structure is what capstone teams will produce as Deliverable 1. It pairs directly with the Annex 1 project plan template from Week 1 — they cover the same ground from two different angles. The more you internalize this structure now, the easier Week 14 gets.

02 · Project portfolio management

Most organizations are running more projects than they think10 min

Ask any IT director to list all the active projects their team is working on. They'll usually say 10 or 15. The actual number — once you count the informal favors, the "quick fixes" that have been running for three months, the migrations someone started on the side — is often twice that.

Portfolio management brings all of this into the open and makes it manageable. Once you can see everything, you can prioritize properly.

Three aspects of portfolio management (§2.3)
Define
Portfolio definition
What projects are currently running? What is proposed? Build an accurate inventory first — you can't manage what you can't see.
Manage
Portfolio management
Prioritize, resource, and coordinate the active set. When resources are constrained — and they always are — this is where the hard trade-offs get made explicitly rather than by accident.
Optimize
Portfolio optimization
Balance the mix between new product development (NPD — things you build for customers or revenue) and renewal projects (improving how the organization operates). A healthy portfolio has both. A portfolio with only renewal projects means the organization is improving itself but not growing. Only NPD means infrastructure rots.

The "below the line" problem: informal, ad-hoc work that doesn't appear in the official portfolio but still consumes real staff time and budget. Getting this work into the portfolio is often the most valuable thing a portfolio management initiative achieves — not because the work stops, but because now leadership can make conscious decisions about whether it should continue.

03 · Evaluating individual projects

Can we build it? Should we?10 min

Every project proposal needs to survive two independent tests before work begins. Failing either test should stop the project — or at least trigger a redesign of the proposal.

Technical assessment
Can the required functionality be achieved with current, affordable technology? Some things are technically possible but require hardware or software so expensive that the cost-benefit analysis fails instantly. Others require technology that doesn't exist yet. This filter should be applied first — there's no point doing financial analysis on a technically impossible proposal.
Cost-benefit analysis
Identify every cost (development, setup, training, ongoing operation, eventual decommissioning) and every benefit — then express them all in monetary terms. Figure 2.1 shows what this typically looks like over time: cash flow is negative during development (spending without earning), turns positive once the system is operating, and may go negative again at the end of life when decommissioning costs arrive.
Exercise 2.1 — Brightmouth College (in-class, 5 min)

For the payroll replacement project, identify as many costs and benefits as you can and sort them into three buckets:

  • Quantified and valued — can be expressed as a specific monetary figure (e.g. "£12,000/year currently paid to the local authority")
  • Quantified but not valued — measurable but not easily converted to money (e.g. "reduces processing errors by 40%")
  • Identified but not easily valued — genuinely real but resists quantification (e.g. "improved employee confidence in pay accuracy")

Good discussion question: which bucket tends to be most underestimated? And what happens when you ignore the third bucket entirely in your business case?

04 · Cost-benefit evaluation techniques

Four ways to measure financial value — each reveals something different20 min

Here's the trap: two projects can look identical on one measure and very different on another. Real evaluation uses several techniques in combination, because each one captures a blind spot the others miss.

Work through Table 2.1 (four IOE project cash flows) live on the board or a spreadsheet — the numbers are small enough to follow by hand.

Technique What it asks Formula Blind spot
Net Profit How much do we make in total, over the whole lifetime? Σ income − Σ costs Ignores when cash flows happen. £50k in year 1 and £50k in year 5 look exactly the same.
Payback Period How long until we've recovered our investment? Read from cumulative cash flow table — the year cumulative goes positive Completely ignores everything that happens after break-even. A project that pays back in year 3 then earns nothing for 7 years looks great.
ROI / ARR What's our average annual return as a percentage? (avg annual profit ÷ total investment) × 100 Still ignores timing. Harder to compare with a bank interest rate, because they compound.
NPV What are all future cash flows worth in today's money? PV = value ÷ (1 + r)ᵗ — summed over all years Requires choosing a discount rate — the answer is sensitive to that choice.
IRR What interest rate would make NPV exactly zero? Solve iteratively (use a spreadsheet IRR function) Doesn't show absolute size. Can have multiple solutions for complex cash flow patterns.
Why NPV is the most important of these

Net profit treats all time periods equally, which isn't realistic. £1,000 five years from now is not the same as £1,000 today — if you had £1,000 today, you could put it in the bank and it would grow. NPV adjusts for this by discounting future cash flows back to their present value, using your organization's "cost of capital" (or required rate of return) as the discount rate.

The result: two projects with identical net profit can rank very differently on NPV, simply because one pays returns earlier. Exercises 2.5–2.6 demonstrate this exactly with the IOE projects.

05 · Risk evaluation

Two different kinds of "this could go wrong"10 min

There are two very different types of risk in any project, and confusing them leads to poor decisions. They need different responses, managed by different people.

Project risk
Threatens the execution of the project — delays, overruns, technical failures during the build. This is managed by the PM during the project. We'll cover this in depth in Week 7.
Business risk
Threatens whether the delivered product will actually be profitable. A project can be delivered perfectly on time and on budget — and still fail because a competitor launched first, or the market shifted, or the expected efficiency gains didn't materialize. This is the focus of §2.6, and it's what the business case needs to address.
Risk matrix — Table 2.5

For each identified business risk, rate it on two dimensions: Importance (how bad if it happens?) and Likelihood (how probable?), each as High / Medium / Low. Risks in the HH corner need active mitigation plans. Risks in the LL corner can just be monitored.

Baking risk into the numbers

For risky projects, the book recommends adding a risk premium (typically 2–5%) to the discount rate used in NPV calculations. Try this in the calculator above — moving from 10% to 15% on Project 1 flips the NPV from positive to negative. That's the mechanism: a higher discount rate says "future cash flows are worth less, because we're less certain they'll arrive."

06 · What is a programme?

When projects need each other to succeed10 min

Imagine a bank that wants to modernize its entire customer experience — online banking, mobile app, branch systems, and back-office processing. That's not one project. It's twenty projects. And they're all interdependent: you can't deliver the mobile app without the new back-office API, can't deliver the branch system without the new customer identity service. None of these projects delivers full value on its own. Together, they transform the bank.

That's a programme.

Ferns' definition: "A group of projects that are managed in a coordinated way to gain benefits that would not be possible were the projects to be managed independently."

Five types of programme
Business cycle programmes
Tied to an annual planning cycle — the full set of projects funded in a given fiscal year. The "portfolio" at the organizational level.
Strategic programmes
All the projects contributing to one strategic goal. Example: everything connected to "become the lowest-cost provider in the market" — every project under that umbrella is part of the programme.
Infrastructure programmes
Renew shared platforms that underpin multiple other things — network upgrades, cloud migrations, ERP replacements. Nothing else can move forward until the infrastructure is ready.
R&D programmes
Open-ended — objectives may evolve as learning accumulates. Unlike projects, the goal itself might change during execution.
Innovative partnerships
Cross-organizational — two or more organizations co-invest in a shared objective (a consortium, a joint venture, an industry standard).
07 · Programme manager vs. project manager

It's a different job, not just a bigger job10 min

Students often assume "programme manager" just means "senior project manager." The roles are genuinely different in how they relate to people, resources, and success. A great project manager who becomes a programme manager for the first time often struggles at first — because the mental model of "own it, deliver it, close it" doesn't transfer.

Programme Manager Project Manager
Manages many simultaneous projectsManages one project at a time
Personal relationship with individual skilled people — knows who's exceptional at whatImpersonal relationship with a resource type (e.g. "I need a senior Java developer")
Needs to maximize utilization of shared resources across the portfolioNeeds to minimize resource demand on their own project
Projects tend to be similar (domain expertise is essential — you need to know the territory)Projects tend to be dissimilar across a PM's career — general management skills transfer more than domain knowledge
Focus: benefit realization over yearsFocus: delivery within a fixed time box

08 · Creating a programme

From "we should do something about this" to a delivery plan10 min

§2.10. Walk through the pipeline using the book's "merging two organizations" example — a bank acquires another bank and needs to integrate all their systems, processes, and people:

01
Programme mandate
Senior leadership decides a strategic change is needed. They appoint a Programme Director. The mandate is the authorization to start planning — it doesn't yet say what the plan is.
02
Programme brief
The high-level definition: what's the vision, what benefits do we expect, what risks are already visible, what are the rough costs and timescales? Enough to decide whether to invest in a full blueprint.
03
Blueprint
The "to be" state in detail: how the business will work when the programme completes — business models, organizational structure, information systems, costs of operating in the new state. This is what all the projects are building toward.
04
Benefit profiles
For each expected benefit: what it is, how it will be measured, when it will arrive, and who is accountable for making sure it actually materializes.
05
Programme portfolio
The set of projects needed to move from the current state to the blueprint. Then: a preliminary delivery plan and a financial plan for the whole programme.
09 · Aids to programme management

Seeing the dependencies and grouping the work10 min

§2.11. Two practical tools that make programme management visible:

Dependency diagram — Figure 2.3
Shows which projects must finish before others can start. The book's example has seven projects (A through G). Projects C and D both depend on A completing first — because A is a systems study that both workstreams need as input. If A slips, everything slips. The dependency diagram makes these bottlenecks visible so you can manage them proactively.
Tranches — Figure 2.4
Grouping the portfolio into tranches — packages of projects that together deliver a coherent set of benefits. Each tranche ends with a decision gate: is the programme still on track? Still worth continuing? Should we adjust the blueprint? This makes a programme reviewable and stoppable at logical points, rather than being committed to the full plan no matter what happens.
10 · Reservations and benefits management

The benefits don't arrive on go-live day5 min

Here's the uncomfortable truth about benefits: they almost never show up inside the project timeline. If you're replacing a payroll system, the savings from fewer errors and faster processing don't arrive on the day you switch it on — they accumulate over months of operation, as people learn the new system, as old workarounds get removed, as the organization changes how it works.

By the time the benefits arrive, the project team has long since moved on. So who's checking? That's the question benefits management tries to answer.

Types of benefit (Table 2.8)
Mandatory compliance
Legal or regulatory — the benefit is avoiding a penalty or keeping the right to operate. Non-negotiable, and usually the easiest to justify.
Quality of service
Faster, more reliable, more accurate delivery to users or customers.
Productivity
Doing more with the same — or fewer — resources.
Risk reduction
Lower probability or lower impact of adverse events.
Economy
Reduced costs. The most directly measurable benefit category.
Revenue enhancement / acceleration
More revenue, or the same revenue arriving sooner (which has NPV implications).
Strategic fit
Better position for future opportunities — the hardest to quantify, but often the real reason a programme was started.
Don't forget disbenefits

Not every consequence is positive. Disbenefits are negative outcomes that are an expected and predictable result of the change — for example: a new sales system drives more revenue, which creates more orders, which creates more overtime cost in the fulfilment team. That overtime cost is a disbenefit. Include it in the business case, or it will appear as a surprise later and make the programme look like it underperformed.

Business change managers own benefit realization after the projects close. This is the crucial structural insight: the people who build a system cannot also track whether it delivered its promised benefits — because those benefits emerge months or years later in operations. Someone outside the project team must be explicitly accountable for this. Otherwise the benefits are aspirational, not real.

Reservations (§2.12): A programme isn't a "super-project." Over-structuring risks creating bureaucracy that prevents individual project managers from responding to change. Hold the programme structure firmly enough to ensure coordination, but loosely enough that it can adapt as the business environment changes.

11 · Wrap-up

Four things to take away

1. Evaluate on multiple grounds, not just financial
A project can be financially strong but technically infeasible. Or financially borderline but strategically essential. Strategic fit, technical feasibility, and economic return are all three required.
2. Some projects are only justifiable as part of a programme
If a project can't deliver value on its own — only in combination with enabling projects — it belongs in a programme. Evaluating it in isolation will either under-value it (by missing the combined benefit) or over-approve it (by assuming the enabling projects will just happen).
3. Not all benefits can be precisely quantified — and that's OK
A good business case names and acknowledges non-quantifiable benefits rather than pretending they don't exist. Dropping them because they're hard to measure leads to systematic under-investment in the work that matters most strategically.
4. Assess the full system lifetime, not just development cost
Operational costs over 10 years routinely dwarf initial build costs. NPV forces this discipline by requiring all future cash flows to be enumerated. Without it, organizations systematically underestimate the true cost of what they're buying.
