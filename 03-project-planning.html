An Overview of Project Planning

Planning isn't something you do once at the start of a project. It's something you do continuously, at multiple levels, adjusting as reality diverges from expectation. This week: a step-by-step framework for doing it right.

01 · What a plan actually is

The word "plan" hides a lot of confusion10 min

When a manager says "we need a plan," they often mean a schedule — a list of tasks with dates. That's one part of a plan, but it's not the whole thing. A real project plan is more like a complete argument for how the work will get done: it describes what needs to happen, in what order, using what resources, at what cost, with what risks, and how you'll know when it's working.

The other confusion is between a plan (the document) and planning (the activity). The document goes stale almost immediately — reality rarely matches the initial assumptions. But the activity of planning — regularly asking "where are we, where do we need to be, what's in the way?" — is what actually keeps a project on track.

Annex A of Hughes & Cotterell lists what a project plan document should contain: Introduction, Background, Objectives, Constraints, Methods, Products, Activities, Resources, Risks, Management approach. This is the template for your capstone Deliverable 1 — and it maps directly to the 10-part business case from Week 2.

Three types of plan in a typical project
Project plan
Covers the entire project from start to finish. High-level. Stable enough to share with stakeholders and use as the baseline for control.
Stage plan
A detailed plan for the next phase of work. More granular than the project plan — covers the next 4–8 weeks at the task level. Updated at the start of each phase.
Exception plan
Created when a stage goes significantly off track. Replaces the current stage plan with a new one that accounts for what actually happened.
02 · Step Wise

A framework for turning a project into a plan30 min

Hughes & Cotterell introduce their own planning framework, called Step Wise. It's not a methodology for doing the project — it's a methodology for planning any project, regardless of what methodology you use to execute it.

The framework has two loops: an outer loop that creates the programme-level plan for the whole project, and an inner loop that repeats for each individual activity as the project progresses. Click each step below to see what it involves:

0
Select project
This step is the output of Chapter 2 — the business case and portfolio management process. By the time you're planning, the project has already been approved. Key question: do we have a clear mandate and committed funding?
1
Identify project scope and objectives
What exactly will this project deliver — and what will it not deliver? Without explicit scope boundaries, everything is up for grabs and the project never ends. Outputs: Statement of Work, objectives (SMART), measurable success criteria.
2
Identify project infrastructure
What's already in place that this project fits into? Organizational standards, existing systems, reporting structures, tools, physical environments. Key question: what constraints does our environment impose before we even start planning?
3
Analyze project characteristics
What kind of project is this? (Recall Week 1's spectrum: routine → project → exploration.) Is it objective-driven or product-driven? Information system or embedded? What does that imply for the right methodology? Output: choice of project approach — sets up Chapter 4.
4
Identify products and activities
What tangible things will the project produce (products/deliverables)? What work is needed to produce each one (activities)? This is the Work Breakdown Structure — covered in detail in Week 6. Note: always start from deliverables, not tasks. Tasks are how you get there; deliverables are what you're building.
5
Estimate effort for each activity
How long will each activity take? This is notoriously difficult in software — covered fully in Weeks 5a/5b. At the planning stage, all estimates have uncertainty. The plan must make that uncertainty visible, not hide it in false precision.
6
Identify activity risks
What could go wrong with each specific activity? (Not project risks broadly — those were in Week 2. These are risks at the task level: "the database migration might take twice as long if the legacy data is messier than we expect.") Risk identification at this level feeds back into estimates. Covered in Week 7.
7
Allocate resources
Assign people, equipment, and budget to each activity. Resources are always constrained — this step turns the abstract plan into a schedule anchored in reality. Who is actually available, and when? Covered in Week 8.
8
Review and publicize the plan
A plan that hasn't been reviewed is a guess. A plan that hasn't been shared is useless. Review: does it make sense? Are the estimates realistic? Are the risks identified? Publicize: every stakeholder who needs to know about it should have seen it and signed off. A plan without buy-in is just a wish list.
↓ inner loop — repeats for each activity
9
Execute the plan / initiate lower-level planning
Work begins. As each activity is approached, it gets its own mini-plan (Steps 4–8 applied again at finer granularity). The outer plan gives direction; the inner loop keeps daily work grounded in reality. Monitoring and control happen continuously here — covered in Week 9.

The most important property of Step Wise: it's iterative. Steps 5, 6, and 7 feed back into each other — if estimates in step 5 reveal the project can't be done in the available time, you return to step 4 and remove scope. If resource allocation in step 7 reveals a key person isn't available, the timeline shifts and risks may change. Planning is negotiation between objectives, resources, and time.

03 · Constraints and the plan

The things that aren't negotiable shape everything else15 min

Every project operates within constraints. Some of those constraints are explicitly stated ("the system must be live before the fiscal year end"). Others are implicit — the team might not even realize they're there until they've already committed to a schedule that violates them.

The plan's job is to make all constraints visible and accounted for.

Technical constraints
The system must integrate with an existing API. It must run on hardware the client already owns. It must meet a specific performance benchmark. These are design constraints that limit the solution space before any design decisions are made.
Quality constraints
Acceptable defect rates, compliance standards, test coverage requirements. In safety-critical systems, these can dominate the entire plan — testing takes longer than building.
Resource constraints
Budget caps, team size limits, specific skill requirements. The classic tension: a shorter timeline requires more people, but more people often slow things down (Brooks' Law — covered in Week 11).
Schedule constraints
Fixed external deadlines — a regulatory deadline, a product launch tied to a marketing campaign, a client go-live date. When these exist, scope and resources become the only variables that can flex.

04 · Products and activities

Start with what you're building, not what you're doing15 min

One of the most common planning mistakes: building a task list before thinking about deliverables. Tasks are easy to generate — you can always add more. But if you start from the list of things the project must produce, the tasks become much more grounded in what actually matters.

PBS
Product Breakdown Structure
A hierarchical list of everything the project must deliver. Start at the top with the final product, then break it down into components and sub-components. This is the "what."
WBS
Work Breakdown Structure
A hierarchical list of all the work needed to produce the PBS. Derived from the PBS, not invented independently. Each leaf node in the WBS should correspond to a deliverable in the PBS. This is the "how."
PFD
Product Flow Diagram
Shows how products are created from other products — which deliverables are inputs to which other deliverables. Reveals the natural sequencing of work without needing to think about resources yet.

05 · Wrap-up

What Step Wise gives you

1. A structure for the plan, not just the project
Step Wise tells you what a plan needs to contain, in what order to figure it out, and how the pieces connect. Without this, planning becomes a free-form conversation that ends in a schedule with no foundation.
2. The two-level loop
The outer loop creates the programme plan. The inner loop replans each activity just before it starts — when you have much better information. Planning too far ahead in detail is wasted effort; Step Wise structures when to plan what.
3. A connective thread through the course
Every remaining chapter of this course covers one or more of the Step Wise steps in depth. Estimation (Week 5) = Step 5. Risk (Week 7) = Step 6. Resource allocation (Week 8) = Step 7. Monitoring (Week 9) = Step 9. Step Wise is the skeleton the course hangs on.
