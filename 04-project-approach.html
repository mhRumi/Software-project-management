Selecting a Project Approach

Waterfall. Agile. Prototyping. Incremental. Everyone has an opinion about which is best. The real answer is: it depends on the project. This week we build the framework for making that choice deliberately, not by habit.

01 · Why the approach matters

The approach is a bet about what you know10 min

Every project approach makes an implicit assumption about certainty. Waterfall assumes you can fully define requirements up front. Agile assumes you can't. Prototyping assumes users don't know what they want until they see something. Which assumption matches your project's reality? That's how you choose.

Getting this wrong is expensive. Choose waterfall for a project where requirements will inevitably change, and you'll spend months building the wrong thing and then fighting change requests. Choose an agile approach for a safety-critical system where every decision needs sign-off, and you'll spend sprints re-opening settled issues.

Step Wise Step 3 (from last week) is where this decision gets made. The output of that step is your chosen approach. This week we give you the information to make that choice well.

02 · The major approaches

Compare them for your specific project30 min

Click each approach to see when it works, when it doesn't, and what it requires:

Build-and-fix
No lifecycle model · the default when you have no process

Code first, think later. Build something, show it to the user, fix what's wrong, repeat. No formal requirements. No design phase. This isn't really a methodology — it's the absence of one. And most projects start here by default unless someone actively introduces a process.

Works when
The project is tiny (<1 person, <1 week), well understood, and the "client" is the developer themselves.
Fails when
Anyone else is involved, the project has a deadline, or quality matters. Technical debt accumulates instantly and compounds.
Core risk
None on paper — very fast to start.
Core risk
No plan → no control → unpredictable delivery. Maintenance becomes impossible.
Waterfall
Sequential · each phase completes before the next begins

Requirements → Design → Build → Test → Deploy. Each phase produces a signed-off document before the next begins. The metaphor is a waterfall: water flows in one direction — you can't go back upstream easily. This is the most common "traditional" approach and maps closely to the ISO 12207 lifecycle from Week 1.

Works when
Requirements are fully understood and stable. The technology is known. Clients need formal documentation and sign-off. Government or defence contracts.
Fails when
Requirements change mid-project (they almost always do). Users can't articulate what they need without seeing it. The technology is novel.
Strengths
Clear milestones. Easy to estimate. Full documentation at each stage. Progress is measurable.
Weaknesses
Late integration of all components. Bugs found late are expensive. Inflexible to change. Users see nothing until the end.
V-Model
Waterfall + explicit testing phases paired with each development phase

An evolution of waterfall. Each development phase on the left of the "V" has a corresponding test phase on the right. Requirements define acceptance tests. Architecture defines integration tests. Detailed design defines unit tests. The tests are planned early, not as an afterthought.

Works when
Quality and correctness are paramount. Safety-critical systems. Medical, aviation, defence.
Adds over waterfall
Test planning happens alongside development planning — not as an afterthought after the code is written.
Strengths
Explicit validation at every stage. Defects found earlier. Traceability between requirements and tests.
Weaknesses
Even more rigid than waterfall. Very heavy documentation overhead. Slow to deliver working software.
Prototyping
Build to learn · show the user something so they can tell you what they actually want

Build a quick, rough version of part of the system — just enough to make the requirements conversation concrete. Show it to users. They'll react to what they see rather than to an abstract description. Use that reaction to clarify the requirements, then build the real thing. The prototype is almost always thrown away — its purpose is learning, not delivery.

Works when
Users genuinely don't know what they want until they see it. Novel UI concepts. Requirements are fuzzy. High risk of building the wrong thing.
Critical warning
Don't let the prototype become the production system. It was built to explore, not to be maintained.
Strengths
Rapidly surfaces misunderstandings. Users feel heard. Reduces costly late-stage rework.
Weaknesses
Can give users unrealistic expectations about delivery speed. Hard to manage scope creep once users start requesting changes to the prototype.
Incremental delivery
Deliver a working subset · expand it in successive releases

Instead of delivering everything at once, deliver a useful subset of the system early, then expand functionality in subsequent releases. Each increment is fully working and tested. Users get value sooner and can give feedback that informs the next increment. The final product is built in layers rather than assembled at the end.

Works when
Some features are more urgent than others. Early delivery has business value. Requirements are likely to evolve based on user experience.
Architecture requirement
The system architecture must support incremental growth from the start — adding increment 3 shouldn't require rewriting increment 1.
Strengths
Early ROI. Real user feedback. Lower risk of total failure. Easier to stop if business conditions change.
Weaknesses
Architecture planning is critical and difficult. Users may not accept a "partial" system. Coordination complexity between increments.
Scrum
Agile · fixed-length sprints with continuous feedback and adaptation

Work is organized into short sprints (1–4 weeks). Each sprint takes items from a prioritized backlog, builds them, tests them, and delivers a potentially shippable product increment. The backlog is re-prioritized at the start of every sprint — so the most valuable work is always next. Scrum doesn't assume you can plan the future; it assumes you'll learn your way there.

Key roles
Product Owner: owns the backlog, decides priority. Scrum Master: removes blockers, protects the team. Development Team: self-organizes to deliver each sprint.
Key ceremonies
Sprint Planning → Daily Standup → Sprint Review → Sprint Retrospective. Each has a specific purpose and time limit.
Works when
Requirements will change. Users can engage continuously. The team is small and co-located. Speed and adaptability are more important than predictability.
Struggles when
Fixed-price contracts require a fully-defined scope. Safety-critical systems require formal sign-off at each step. Clients need to see a detailed plan before agreeing to fund.
03 · How to choose

The right approach depends on what you know — and what you don't20 min

There's no universally best approach. The choice depends on the nature of the project, the organization's culture, the contract type, and the team's skills. Here are the key questions to ask:

How well understood are the requirements?
If requirements are clear and stable → waterfall or V-model. If requirements are fuzzy or likely to change → agile or prototyping. If the goal is clear but the solution isn't → incremental delivery.
What are the quality and safety requirements?
Safety-critical systems (medical, aviation, defence) almost always require the V-model or a similarly rigorous approach. Every decision needs traceability and sign-off. Agile's flexibility is a liability here, not an asset.
How available are the users?
Agile and prototyping both require frequent, meaningful user involvement. If your client can only meet quarterly, scrum doesn't work — the sprint reviews will be superficial and the backlog will drift.
What type of contract is in place?
A fixed-price contract requires a defined scope — which maps to waterfall. A time-and-materials contract can accommodate changing scope — which enables agile. (Contract types covered in Week 10.)
What's the team's experience with the technology?
If the team has done this exact thing before, a sequential approach works. If the technology is new, plan for experimentation — incremental delivery or spikes (short research tasks) built into the schedule.

The hybrid reality: most real projects use a hybrid. Requirements might be gathered using agile techniques (user stories, workshops), documented using structured methods (use cases, specifications), built using incremental delivery, and tested using V-model discipline. Don't treat these as mutually exclusive religions — treat them as tools you combine based on need.

04 · Agile in more depth

What "agile" actually means in practice15 min

The word "agile" gets overloaded. It's a value system (the Agile Manifesto, 2001), a family of methodologies (Scrum, XP, Kanban, SAFe), and also a buzzword that organisations apply to whatever they're already doing. Let's unpack what it actually means for project management.

The Agile Manifesto — four values
Individuals and interactions over processes and tools. Working software over comprehensive documentation. Customer collaboration over contract negotiation. Responding to change over following a plan. The manifesto doesn't say process, documentation, contracts, and plans are bad — it says the items on the left matter more when they conflict with the items on the right.
Scrum — the sprint cycle
Sprint Planning (commit to a backlog slice) → daily Standups (15-min syncs: what I did, what I'll do, what's blocking me) → Sprint Review (demo to stakeholders) → Sprint Retrospective (what went well, what to improve). Then repeat. The rhythm is the structure — it replaces detailed upfront planning with continuous replanning.
XP — Extreme Programming
Pair programming, test-driven development (write tests before code), continuous integration, refactoring, and very short release cycles. More technical and prescriptive than Scrum — focused on code quality rather than project management. Often used alongside Scrum (Scrum for the process, XP for the engineering practices).
Agile at scale — the PM challenge

Scrum works beautifully for a single team of 5–9 people. Now imagine 10 teams all working on the same product. Who coordinates the backlogs? How do you avoid one team's sprint creating a dependency that blocks three others?

Scaling frameworks like SAFe (Scaled Agile Framework) and LeSS (Large-Scale Scrum) try to solve this. The honest answer is that large-scale agile is genuinely hard — and many organizations that claim to be "doing agile at scale" are actually doing something closer to waterfall with sprint names attached.

05 · Wrap-up

The key insight

Methodology choice is a risk management decision
Choosing an approach is really choosing which risks to accept and which to mitigate. Waterfall accepts the risk of late discovery in exchange for upfront clarity. Agile accepts the risk of scope ambiguity in exchange for flexibility. Know what you're trading off before you commit.
The approach shapes the contract
Or vice versa. The relationship between the project approach and the contract type is tight — you can't run scrum under a fixed-price contract without one of them breaking. This becomes a recurring theme in Week 10.
Document the reasons for your choice
When someone asks later "why are we doing it this way?", you need an answer grounded in project characteristics, not habit or fashion. Step 3 of Step Wise produces this rationale — write it down.
