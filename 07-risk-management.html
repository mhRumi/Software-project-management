Risk Management

A risk is something that might go wrong. Managing risk doesn't mean preventing everything bad from happening — it means making conscious decisions about which bad things to guard against and which to accept. This week: how to do that systematically.

01 · What risk is — and isn't

Risk is uncertainty that matters10 min

A risk is not a problem — it's something that might become a problem. This distinction matters because the appropriate response is different. A problem needs solving now. A risk needs managing before it becomes a problem.

Also: risks are value-neutral until prioritized. The fact that a key developer might leave is a risk — but so is the fact that the market for your product might grow faster than expected. We mostly think of risks as threats, but they can also be opportunities. Real risk management covers both.

Risk = Probability × Impact
The two dimensions that determine a risk's importance: how likely is it to happen, and how bad would it be if it did? A risk that's highly likely but trivially impactful gets less attention than one that's unlikely but catastrophic. Risk exposure = P × I, expressed in time, money, or both.
Residual risk
The risk that remains after you've applied your mitigation strategy. No mitigation eliminates risk entirely — it only reduces probability or impact. The residual risk is what you're still accepting.
Risk appetite
How much residual risk an organization is willing to accept. A startup might accept high risk in exchange for speed. A hospital cannot accept the same risk in patient record software. Know your organization's appetite before setting the threshold for "acceptable residual risk."
02 · Risk identification

You can't manage risks you haven't named20 min

The most common mistake in risk management is not doing enough identification. Teams often list the obvious risks (key person leaves, requirements change) and stop there. The risks that kill projects are usually the ones that weren't on the list.

Checklists
Use a standard list of risk categories as a prompt. The book provides a comprehensive checklist covering: organizational, project management, technical, and external risks. Work through it systematically before starting each new project — different team members will flag different items.
Brainstorming
Gather the full team. Go around the room — everyone contributes one risk at a time, no criticism allowed. The goal is quantity first, quality second. Use checklists to prime the pump if the group gets stuck.
Assumption analysis
Every estimate and every plan rests on assumptions ("the database performance will be adequate," "the API vendor will maintain the current interface"). List those assumptions explicitly. Each one is a potential risk — if the assumption turns out to be wrong, what happens?
Causal mapping (fishbone/Ishikawa)
For complex risk areas, draw a cause-and-effect diagram. Start with the potential bad outcome in the "head" and trace back through categories of cause (People, Process, Technology, External). Surfaces root causes rather than just symptoms.
Common software project risk categories (Boehm)
Personnel shortfalls
Key people are unavailable, have weaker skills than assumed, or leave mid-project. Often the highest-ranked risk in software projects — and the hardest to mitigate without organizational change.
Unrealistic schedule/budget
The constraints were set by management before the team estimated. The plan says 6 months; the team knows it's a year. This risk usually isn't surfaced until it's too late.
Developing the wrong system
Requirements were wrong, misunderstood, or changed. One of the most expensive risks to realize late — entire modules may need to be rebuilt from scratch.
Technology risk
The chosen technology doesn't perform as expected, isn't supported, or has integration incompatibilities only discovered during build.
External risks
Third-party APIs change, suppliers go out of business, regulatory requirements shift, market conditions change. The PM often has zero direct control here — the response is monitoring and contingency planning.

04 · Risk responses

Four things you can do about a risk15 min

Once a risk is assessed, you choose a response strategy. The right strategy depends on the risk's probability, impact, and the cost of responding:

Avoid → eliminate the risk entirely
Change the plan so the risk can't happen. Don't use the unproven technology — use something familiar instead. Don't attempt the aggressive deadline — negotiate a longer one. Cost: usually requires changing scope, timeline, or approach. Use when: the risk is severe and avoidance is cheaper than mitigation.
Reduce → lower probability or impact
Invest in activities that make the risk less likely or less damaging if it occurs. Train the team on the new technology (reduces probability of technical failure). Build a prototype first (reduces probability of building the wrong thing). Keep documentation current (reduces impact of losing a key person). Cost: adds effort upfront. Use when: avoidance is too costly but the risk is significant.
Transfer → move it to someone else
Insurance, fixed-price contracts with suppliers, outsourcing risky components. Note: transfer doesn't eliminate the risk — it changes who bears the cost if it occurs. And transferred risks often come back in disguised forms (vendor failure, contract disputes). Use when: a third party is better placed to absorb or manage the risk.
Accept → do nothing, but be aware
Some risks are cheap enough to absorb if they occur, rare enough that mitigation is wasteful, or impossible to avoid. Document the decision — passive acceptance is a choice, not an oversight. Active acceptance: build a contingency reserve (time or budget) specifically for this risk. Use when: the risk is Low-Low or the cost of any other response exceeds the expected cost of the risk materializing.

05 · Risk monitoring

Risk management is continuous, not a one-off10 min

A risk register written in week 1 and filed away isn't risk management — it's risk theater. Real risk management means revisiting the register regularly: are old risks still relevant? Have new risks emerged? Have any risks materialized and become issues?

Risk triggers (early warning indicators)
For each major risk, identify observable signals that the risk is increasing in probability. "A developer hasn't pushed code in three days" might be an early indicator of disengagement. "The client hasn't responded to requirements questions in two weeks" might signal scope uncertainty growing. Triggers let you act before the risk materializes.
Risk vs. Issue
A risk is something that might happen. An issue is something that has happened. When a risk materializes, it moves from the risk register to an issue log. Issues need immediate action; risks need ongoing monitoring. Don't confuse the two — they require different responses.
Risk review cadence
At minimum, review the risk register at every project milestone or sprint review. In high-risk phases (integration, testing, cutover), review weekly. The PM should maintain a short-list of the top 5 current risks — these are the things that keep them awake, and the things they should be watching most carefully.
