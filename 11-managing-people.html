Managing People

Software is built by people. Not processes, not tools, not methodologies — people. Everything else in this course is a support structure for the humans doing the work. This week: what makes people productive, motivated, and willing to stick around.

01 · Why this matters

The best process won't save you from a disengaged team10 min

DeMarco and Lister measured software team productivity across dozens of organizations and found that the best teams were 10× more productive than the worst — not 10% more. That gap wasn't explained by technology, process, or experience level. It was explained almost entirely by team cohesion, psychological safety, and whether people actually wanted to do the work.

Managing people isn't soft — it's the highest-leverage thing a PM can do. Understanding what motivates the people on your team, what stresses them, and what keeps them engaged is not optional. It's the job.

02 · Maslow's hierarchy of needs

You can't meet a higher need until lower ones are covered20 min

Abraham Maslow's hierarchy (1943) describes human needs in five levels. The key insight for management: people are motivated by whatever need is currently unmet. You can't inspire someone with "meaningful work" if they're worried about keeping their job.

03 · Herzberg's two-factor theory

Removing dissatisfaction ≠ creating satisfaction15 min

Frederick Herzberg found that job satisfaction and job dissatisfaction are driven by different factors — not opposite ends of the same scale. This is one of the most practically useful — and most misunderstood — findings in management research.

Hygiene factors → cause dissatisfaction if absent
Salary, working conditions, company policy, supervision style, job security, status. If these are poor, people are unhappy. But improving them only removes dissatisfaction — it doesn't create motivation. Giving someone a raise when they're already reasonably paid produces a temporary good feeling, then returns to baseline. Hygiene factors are the price of admission.
Motivators → create genuine satisfaction and engagement
Achievement, recognition for good work, the work itself being interesting, responsibility, advancement, growth. These are intrinsic to the job — you can't install them with a policy. Motivators are what makes people stay late willingly and care about quality.

The practical implication: if you want to motivate a software team, first get the hygiene factors right (fair pay, decent equipment, reasonable deadlines, clear direction). Then create the conditions for motivators: give people real responsibility, let them see the impact of their work, ensure achievements get recognized publicly. Throwing money at a team that's bored and unchallenged won't fix the problem.

04 · McGregor's Theory X and Theory Y

What you believe about people shapes how you manage them15 min

Douglas McGregor argued that every manager holds one of two implicit sets of beliefs about their team — and those beliefs determine their management style, often unconsciously.

X
Theory X — people avoid work if they can
Employees are inherently lazy and dislike responsibility. Left unsupervised, they'll do the minimum. Management must control, direct, and coerce. Leads to: close monitoring, detailed task assignments, approval bottlenecks, distrust. Creates the very behaviour it assumes — being treated as untrustworthy tends to produce untrustworthy behaviour over time.
Y
Theory Y — people seek meaning and take pride in good work
Given the right conditions, employees are self-directed, creative, and willing to take responsibility. Management's job is to create those conditions — clear goals, autonomy to achieve them, recognition when they do. Leads to: delegated responsibility, collaborative goal-setting, investment in people's growth. Creates the self-motivated team it assumes — trust generates trustworthy behaviour.

For software teams specifically: developers are almost always Theory Y people. They chose a demanding, constantly-evolving field because they find it interesting. Managing them with Theory X assumptions — micromanaging, requiring permission for every decision, treating them as productivity units — is one of the fastest ways to lose good people to competitors who treat them differently.

05 · Stress and health

Stress is a project risk, not a personal weakness10 min

Software projects create stress — tight deadlines, unclear requirements, technical complexity, stakeholder pressure. Some stress is productive (it drives urgency). Chronic stress is destructive — it impairs judgment, causes errors, increases turnover, and can cause serious health problems.

The PM's responsibility is to recognize the difference and to address the organizational causes of stress, not just the symptoms.

Common causes in software projects
Unrealistic deadlines. Unclear or constantly-changing requirements. Lack of technical support or tooling. Poor communication from management. Being asked to deliver quality while being given insufficient time. Feeling responsible for outcomes you don't control.
Signs to watch for
Declining code quality. Increasing error rates. Reduced communication. Withdrawal from team activities. Presenteeism (being present but not productive). Brittle tempers in meetings. The PM who only looks at deliverables will miss these signals until it's too late.
What helps
Honest estimates with stakeholders (don't accept impossible deadlines silently). Regular 1-on-1s where people can raise concerns. Recognition of achievements, not just escalation of problems. Flexible working where possible. Ensuring workloads are visible — overload shouldn't be invisible.
