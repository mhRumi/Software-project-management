Working in Teams

Putting five skilled individuals in a room doesn't make a team. Teams go through predictable stages of development, carry distinct roles, and make decisions in ways that can either leverage everyone's strengths or amplify everyone's biases. This week: how teams actually work.

01 · How teams develop

Every new team goes through the same stages25 min

Imagine you've just been assigned to a project team of four people you've never worked with. On day one, everyone is polite, a bit cautious, and deferring to each other. Three weeks in, there's an argument about how to approach a design decision. Two months in, the team has its own shorthand and just gets things done.

This isn't random. Psychologist Bruce Tuckman identified in 1965 that all groups go through the same sequence of stages, and later added a fifth. Understanding where your team is in this sequence determines what kind of leadership they actually need.

Forming
Polite · Uncertain · Dependent on leader

The team has just come together. People are sizing each other up, avoiding conflict, testing the boundaries of acceptable behaviour. There is high dependence on the leader for direction — nobody yet knows what the norms are.

What you see
Lots of questions, requests for clarification. Excessive politeness. People holding back opinions. Waiting for others to go first.
What the PM should do
Be directive. Clarify goals, roles, and expectations explicitly. Create quick early wins. Introductions matter — help people see each other as humans, not resources.
Storming
Friction · Testing authority · Sub-groups forming

The politeness wears off. Conflicts emerge — about how to work, who has authority, different working styles. This feels like the team is breaking down. It isn't. It's growing. Storming is necessary — a team that skips it has suppressed its conflicts, not resolved them.

What you see
Disagreements about approach. Resistance to the PM's direction. Cliques forming. Productivity dips temporarily.
What the PM should do
Coach, don't control. Create space for disagreements to surface constructively. Don't suppress conflict — help the team work through it. Focus on process, not personality.
Norming
Agreement · Cohesion · Shared norms

The team has worked through its conflicts and established shared ways of working. Trust is building. People understand each other's strengths and compensate for weaknesses without being asked. There's a team identity now — an "us."

What you see
More collaboration, less friction. Shared language and shorthand. Constructive feedback. Rising productivity.
What the PM should do
Support and facilitate. Delegate more, intervene less. Reinforce positive norms. Watch for early signs of groupthink as cohesion increases.
Performing
High output · Autonomous · Self-correcting

Not all teams reach this stage. Those that do are remarkable — highly functional, self-directing, and capable of complex problem-solving with minimal overhead. The team can manage its own dynamics. Members know when to lead and when to follow without being told.

What you see
High output, high quality. Self-organised. Disagreements resolved quickly internally. Very little need for PM intervention on day-to-day work.
What the PM should do
Delegate and unblock. Your job is removing obstacles and protecting the team from external interference — not managing the work directly. Get out of the way.
Adjourning
Closure · Reflection · Transition

Added by Tuckman in 1977. When the project ends, the team disbands. This has a real emotional dimension — particularly for high-performing teams. Done poorly, it creates anxiety and disengagement in the final phases. Done well, it becomes a source of motivation for the next assignment.

What you see
Reduced focus on delivery as people worry about what's next. Some disengagement. Emotional responses to project closure — pride, relief, sadness.
What the PM should do
Mark the ending deliberately. Run a retrospective. Celebrate achievements. Be transparent about what happens next. Ensure knowledge is captured before people disperse.

Important: any significant change to the team — a new member joins, a key person leaves, scope changes radically — can send the team back to Forming or Storming. This is normal. The PM should expect it and respond accordingly.

02 · Belbin team roles

The team you want vs. the team you have20 min

Meredith Belbin spent nine years studying management teams at Henley Business School. His central finding: teams of brilliant individuals often perform worse than teams of average individuals with balanced roles. The problem isn't competence — it's that teams of experts tend to have the same blind spots, fight over the same territory, and neglect the same tasks.

Belbin identified nine team roles that need to be covered for a team to function effectively. One person can cover multiple roles, and roles shift depending on context — but if a role is consistently absent, the team has a structural weakness.

Thinking
Plant
Creative, unorthodox. Generates novel ideas but can be impractical. Easily hurt by criticism.
Thinking
Monitor Evaluator
Analytical, strategic. Evaluates options objectively and spots flaws. Can seem unenthusiastic but rarely wrong.
Thinking
Specialist
Deep technical knowledge. Provides rare expertise on specific topics. Tends to stay narrow; may disengage outside their specialism.
Action
Shaper
Driven, challenging, thrives under pressure. Drives the team to overcome obstacles. Can be provocative or abrasive.
Action
Implementer
Disciplined, practical, reliable. Turns ideas into concrete plans and gets on with it. Slow to change; can be inflexible.
Action
Completer Finisher
Meticulous, painstaking, conscientious. Ensures nothing is missed. Can worry unnecessarily; reluctant to delegate.
People
Coordinator
Calm, confident, mature. Clarifies goals and delegates effectively. Can be perceived as manipulative.
People
Teamworker
Cooperative, perceptive, diplomatic. Keeps the team together under pressure. Can be indecisive in tough calls.
People
Resource Investigator
Enthusiastic, outgoing, communicative. Explores opportunities and external connections. Loses interest after initial excitement.
Group Activity — 15 min

In your capstone project group: each person identifies which one or two Belbin roles they naturally tend to play. Map out your team's coverage. Answer these questions:

1. Which roles are covered by multiple people? (risk: duplication, stepping on toes)

2. Which roles are missing entirely? (risk: structural blind spot)

3. Can anyone stretch to cover the missing roles? Or do you need to consciously compensate?

03 · Decision making

How a team decides determines what it can agree to10 min

Not all decisions should be made the same way. The method you choose affects both the quality of the decision and the team's commitment to implementing it.

⬆
Consensus
Everyone must be able to live with the decision — not necessarily their first choice. High commitment, slow process. Best for high-stakes, irreversible decisions where buy-in matters.
✓
Majority vote
Faster than consensus. Risk: the minority may not implement the decision fully. Works when the decision is reversible and commitment from everyone isn't critical.
→
Expert decision
The person with most expertise decides. Fast and well-informed, but disengages everyone else. Best for technical decisions where the rest of the team genuinely can't evaluate the options.
⊙
PM decision (autocratic)
The PM decides and announces. Fastest of all, lowest commitment. Appropriate for urgent, time-pressured decisions where deliberation is too expensive.

Groupthink: high-cohesion teams sometimes converge too quickly on one view, suppressing dissent to preserve harmony. Signs: no disagreement in meetings, alternative views dismissed quickly, illusion of unanimity. Fix it by assigning a devil's advocate, explicitly inviting challenge, or running anonymous idea submissions before group discussion.

04 · Leadership

Leadership style should match the situation10 min

There is no single best leadership style. Hersey and Blanchard's Situational Leadership model argues that the appropriate style depends on the readiness of the team member for the specific task — their competence and commitment level for that work.

Directing low competence, high commitment
New to the task, eager to try. Tell them clearly what to do and how. They need direction, not autonomy — but keep the tone positive. Example: a junior developer on their first deployment.
Coaching some competence, variable commitment
Learning, but encountering setbacks that affect confidence. Explain the why, invite their input, give detailed feedback. More two-way than directing. Example: mid-level dev who's done some of this before but not all of it.
Supporting high competence, variable motivation
They know how to do it but aren't sure they want to. Facilitate rather than direct — share decision-making, listen actively, encourage. The block is motivational, not technical. Example: senior dev who's lost enthusiasm for the project.
Delegating high competence, high commitment
Fully capable and motivated. Set the goal, agree the constraints, then get out of the way. Checking in too frequently signals distrust and reduces performance. Example: a senior dev who's done this a hundred times.
